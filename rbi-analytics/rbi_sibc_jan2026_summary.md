## Section B — Key Insights

### 1. Retail has structurally overtaken corporate as the primary credit engine

Personal Loans hold 33.0% of non-food credit (₹67.2 L Cr) vs Industry's 21.5% (₹43.9 L Cr). Industry's YoY growth (12.1%) trails both Services (15.5%) and Personal Loans (14.9%). Within Industry, the growth differential is stark: Micro & Small grew +31.2% YoY, Medium +22.3%, while Large industry grew only +5.5%. The structural shift from corporate balance-sheet credit to retail/MSME originated product is now visible in the stock data, not just the flow narrative.

**Lending implication:** Products designed around large corporate relationships face a structural headwind. The credit opportunity is in mid-market and retail origination.

### 2. Gold jewellery lending is a system-level signal, not just a product trend

Gold loans grew from ₹0.92 L Cr (Jan 2024) to ₹4.01 L Cr (Jan 2026) — a 337.9% increase in 24 months and 128.8% in the last 12 alone. This growth rate exceeds any other product in the dataset by a factor of 2–3x. Gold prices rose ~25–30% in the same window, but the credit growth far exceeds collateral appreciation. This implies volume expansion: more borrowers pledging gold, not just higher per-unit values.

**Lending implication:** This level of gold-secured credit growth at this speed — primarily from distressed or liquidity-strained borrowers — is a leading indicator of broader household financial stress. It also signals a competitive window before larger banks saturate the category.

### 3. NBFC credit is wholesale funding, not end-borrower reach — the 33% services share is misleading

NBFCs represent 33.3% of services credit (₹19.1 L Cr), making them the single largest services sub-sector. But this is bank-to-NBFC wholesale lending, not bank-to-end-borrower credit. The actual end-borrower sectors being funded through this channel (unsecured consumer, MSME, microfinance) are invisible in the SIBC data. The NBFC share grew 17.8% YoY on a large base, meaning additional ₹2.9 L Cr of credit was intermediated through NBFCs in Jan 2025–Jan 2026.

**Lending implication:** Co-lending, PTC/DA structures, and NBFC-bank partnerships are the mechanism through which banks are currently accessing the high-growth consumer and MSME segments. Direct origination plays in these segments are underrepresented in bank books.

### 4. Credit card growth has effectively stalled under regulatory pressure

Credit card outstanding grew only +1.5% YoY (Jan 2025 to Jan 2026) — from ₹2.92 L Cr to ₹2.96 L Cr. Over the full two-year window, growth was +14.7%, roughly in line with nominal GDP but far below the portfolio's historical 25–30% growth trend. The trajectory shows a plateau: Jan 2024 (₹2.59L), Mar 2024 (₹2.57L), Jan 2025 (₹2.92L), Mar 2025 (₹2.84L), Jan 2026 (₹2.96L).

**Lending implication:** The unsecured credit boom has decelerated meaningfully. Lenders with heavy exposure to revolving unsecured credit cards face margin compression and should evaluate rotation into secured retail (vehicle, gold, housing).

### 5. Infrastructure credit is effectively stalled, masking internal collapse

The Infrastructure aggregate (₹14.3 L Cr, +6.4% YoY) obscures severe internal divergence. Power is the only growing sub-sector (+17.5% YoY, ₹7.89 L Cr). Roads grew just +0.2% (flat in real terms). Telecom contracted -17.2%. Railways -24.2%. Airports -26.1%. Power's strong growth is inflating the headline number — ex-Power, infrastructure credit is declining.

**Lending implication:** Power project finance is the only live infrastructure credit opportunity at scale. All other infrastructure sub-sectors are in deleveraging or capex pause mode.

### 6. All Engineering is the fastest-growing industry segment, driven by supply-chain reshoring

All Engineering (Statement 2) grew +35.9% YoY to ₹3.11 L Cr — the highest YoY growth of any industry sub-sector above ₹1 L Cr. Over two years: +60.3%. Sub-sector breakdown: Other Engineering +38.8% YoY (₹2.47 L Cr), Electronics +25.6% (₹0.64 L Cr). Gems & Jewellery (+35.6% YoY) shows a similar pattern — manufacturing-adjacent sectors with global export linkages are accelerating.

**Lending implication:** Manufacturing supply-chain finance — working capital, machinery loans, export credit — is the highest-velocity opportunity in the corporate segment today.

### 7. Priority Sector Housing is growing at nearly 3x the commercial housing rate

PSL Housing grew +37.9% YoY (₹10.31 L Cr vs ₹7.47 L Cr), while commercial housing loans (₹32.78 L Cr) grew only +11.1% YoY. PSL Housing now represents 31.5% of total housing credit — and growing. Educational Loans under PSL grew +23.5% YoY to ₹0.78 L Cr.

**Lending implication:** Priority sector targets are creating a structural subsidy for lenders who can efficiently originate in the affordable housing segment. The regulatory incentive and the market growth are aligned.

## Section D — Coverage Boundary

This dataset covers **scheduled commercial bank credit** as reported in the RBI SIBC return. It explicitly excludes: (1) NBFC and HFC balance sheets — meaning the ₹19 L Cr of bank-to-NBFC credit represents wholesale funding, not end-borrower exposure; (2) co-lending and securitised pools where credit may have been originated by banks but derecognised from their books; (3) informal and unorganised credit markets; (4) disaggregation of the large "Other Personal Loans" (₹16.85 L Cr, 25.1% of personal loans) and "Other Services" (₹12.36 L Cr) categories; (5) geographic distribution — this is national aggregate data only. The dataset provides 5 date-snapshots (Jan 2024, Mar 2024, Jan 2025, Mar 2025, Jan 2026), which limits time-series analysis to biannual trends and prevents seasonal decomposition with confidence.
