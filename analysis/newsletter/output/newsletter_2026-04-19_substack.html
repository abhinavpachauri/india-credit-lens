Every month the RBI publishes bank credit data across 80+ sectors. India Credit Lens builds a causal model from that data — explaining why credit moved, not just that it moved. This is Issue #2, covering February 2026 data and what the full Jan 2024–Feb 2026 series now reveals.

₹207.5L Cr in total bank credit. FY26 tracking at 13.8% — faster than all of FY25. Three structural forces are now confirmed across multiple data points. One headline number turns out to be a classification event, not growth. And connecting two months of data reveals things no single RBI file can show.

TL;DR

  • ₹207.5L Cr total bank credit — FY26 tracking at 13.8% vs FY25's full-year 11.0%. The system is in genuine structural expansion.
  • PSL Housing +38% is not growth. The Oct 2024 RBI ceiling revision reclassified existing loans already on bank books. ₹2.87L Cr is a relabelling event, not new lending.
  • Gold loans confirmed across three independent data points (+359% over 24 months). MSME corroborated by two independent series. PLI extended to a second cross-section. All three structural shifts are unambiguous.
  • Infrastructure — 32.5% of all industry credit — is growing at its slowest rate. The FY22-24 capex wave is in operations, not drawdown. The next wave (green hydrogen, data centres) is 18-24 months from credit scale.

🗺 The System This Month

Six causal stories now run through India's bank credit system, and February confirms five of them. The RBI's November 2023 risk-weight action is still reshaping retail credit — demand that left credit cards has migrated to gold, secured, RBI-friendly, and still growing. The formalisation wave is now confirmed from two independent series. PLI drawdowns have a second cross-section. One number that looked like a boom — PSL Housing +38% — turns out to be a classification event, not new lending. And stepping back across both months of data: the system is accelerating at 13.8% FY26 while infrastructure, its largest single component, is growing at its slowest rate. These are not contradictions. They are the texture of a credit cycle mid-turn.

📡 The Signals

★ The Acceleration Nobody Computed

New this issue

13.8% FY26 vs 11.0% FY25

FY26 is accelerating above FY25 — invisible in any single RBI file

Total bank credit FY26 is tracking at 13.8% (Feb 2026 vs Mar 2025) against FY25's full-year 11.0%. To compute this you need the Feb 2026 outstanding figure and the Mar 2025 base — which come from two different SIBC files. No single publication contains both. The merged series makes this comparison possible for the first time. The acceleration is not a base effect: it is consistent across both periods in the series.

Any lending portfolio that grew less than 13% in FY26 to February has lost market share in an expanding system. The system is in genuine structural expansion — not post-COVID recovery, not base effect.

✅ EV-specific vehicle credit products

Vehicle loan acceleration is partly EV-driven. EV-specific loan products (battery residual value, charging infrastructure finance) represent a product differentiation opportunity.

First-mover advantage in EV-specific credit is 2-3 years wide. Fleet EV lending (e-commerce, last-mile logistics) is the highest-volume entry point.

★ The Infrastructure Pause

New this issue

+5.8% FY at ₹14.44L Cr — 32.5% of all industry credit

Infrastructure credit is decelerating — the next wave is not yet borrowing

Infrastructure is the largest component of industry credit and it is growing at the slowest rate in the dataset. The FY22-24 capex wave — roads, metro rail, urban utilities — is now in operations. Those projects are repaying, not drawing down. The next wave (green hydrogen, data centres, offshore wind, semiconductor fabs) has received approvals and signed PPAs, but has not yet reached credit drawdown scale. The approval-to-drawdown gap is typically 18-24 months, putting the next infrastructure credit wave at FY27-28.

Corporate banks with heavy infrastructure exposure face a structural growth headwind for the next 12-18 months. The window to build credit capability for the next capex cycle — renewable energy project finance, data centre construction lending — is open now, before the drawdown wave begins.

✅ Renewable energy project finance

Green hydrogen, solar, and battery storage projects represent the next infrastructure capex wave. Banks with project finance capability for renewable energy will capture the credit as the infrastructure supercycle pivots.

No incumbent lender has built renewable energy project finance capabilities at scale. First-movers on DISCOM offtake risk underwriting, distributed solar credit, and battery storage finance will own this market for a decade.

⟳ The Reclassification

Correction from Issue #1

PSL Housing +38%: a number that looks like growth but isn't

What it looked like: Issue #1 flagged the +37.9% surge as suspicious — it reversed two consecutive flat periods with no visible demand catalyst. Three explanations were offered (PMAY acceleration, reclassification, genuine new origination) but left unresolved.

What it actually is: Confirmed reclassification. The Oct 2024 RBI revision raised PSL housing loan ceilings (metro: ₹35L → ₹45L, non-metro: ₹25L → ₹35L). Loans already on bank books that previously exceeded the old limits now qualify as PSL. The ₹2.87L Cr appearing as credit growth is existing exposure being relabelled — not new lending.

Implication: PSL housing achievement figures for FY26 are not comparable to prior years. Any lender or analyst citing +38% PSL housing growth as evidence of market expansion is reading a classification event as an economic signal. The affordable housing opportunity is real — PMAY, Tier 3/4 distribution gaps, strong repayment behaviour — but this particular number cannot be used to size it.
