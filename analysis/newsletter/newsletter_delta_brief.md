# Credit Insights — RBI SIBC Jan 2026

> The 7 most important things the data shows. Each with exact numbers, why it matters now, and one implication for a lender or fintech.

---

## 1. Gold Loans: 4.4x in 24 Months — The Single Biggest Signal in This Data

**The observation:** Loans against gold jewellery grew from ₹0.915L Cr (Jan 2024) to ₹1.75L Cr (Jan 2025) to ₹4.01L Cr (Jan 2026). That's a 338% increase in 24 months. The YoY growth rate itself is accelerating: +91.4% (Jan 2025), +121.1% (Mar 2025), +128.8% (Jan 2026). Gold prices rose roughly 25–30% over the same period. Credit grew 4.4x while the collateral value moved 1.3x. That means far more people are pledging gold, not just the same people pledging more valuable gold.

**Why it matters now:** When large numbers of people pledge their household gold, it typically signals liquidity stress — gold is the lender of last resort for Indian households. This is simultaneously the fastest-growing credit product in retail banking and a stress signal for household financial health. The two facts are not in contradiction. The business opportunity is real. The risk profile of the average gold loan borrower entering the market in 2025–26 is materially different from the borrower who built this market in 2022–23.

**Implication for lenders:** Any lender without a gold loan product is watching a segment grow 10x faster than the market. But entering now requires thinking carefully about who the new borrower is. Income overlay on gold loan underwriting — using GST turnover, UPI flows, salary credits — separates stress-driven borrowers from asset-rich borrowers within the same LTV band. The lenders who price that distinction will do better through the cycle.

---

## 2. Personal Loans Overtook Corporate Credit — And the Gap Is Widening

**The observation:** Personal loans at ₹67.2L Cr (Jan 2026) is now the largest single sector in Indian banking, ahead of Services (₹57.2L Cr), Industry (₹43.9L Cr), and Agriculture (₹25.1L Cr). Personal loans exceeded industry credit in Jan 2024 too (₹52.3L Cr vs ₹36.1L Cr), but the gap has grown: personal loans now exceed industry credit by ₹23.3L Cr, up from ₹16.2L Cr two years ago. Within personal loans, the composition is shifting: gold loans grew 338% while housing credit — 49% of the category — grew just 11.1%.

**Why it matters now:** This is not a new trend, but it has now crossed a threshold where it's the defining structural fact of Indian banking. A bank that allocates more relationship management time to large corporate clients than to retail is allocating effort to a shrinking share of the market. The growth is in retail, and within retail, it's in the faster-moving, shorter-tenor products — gold, vehicle, education — not the slow-moving housing book.

**Implication for lenders:** If you're building a lending business in India in 2026, your baseline assumption should be that retail is the primary market and corporates are secondary. The reverse was true in 2010. The banks that will gain share in the next 5 years are those with efficient retail origination, not larger corporate relationship teams.

---

## 3. Credit Cards Have Hit a Policy Ceiling

**The observation:** Credit card outstanding grew +1.5% YoY in Jan 2026 — after growing 13% in Jan 2025, 25%+ in 2023, and 30%+ in 2022. The category is ₹2.964L Cr, essentially flat from ₹2.921L Cr twelve months earlier. In FY terms (Jan 2026 vs Mar 2025): +4.2%.

**Why it matters now:** This is not cyclical — it is a direct consequence of the RBI's November 2023 risk-weight increase on unsecured consumer credit from 100% to 125%. That one regulatory change made it more capital-expensive for banks to issue and grow credit card portfolios. Banks responded by slowing origination and tightening limits. The regulation is not being reversed; the ceiling will stay. The unsecured credit growth that used to flow through credit cards is not disappearing — it is being re-routed into secured products (vehicle loans: +17.1%, gold: +128.8%) and regulated fintech products.

**Implication for lenders:** Credit card is not a growth product anymore. Banks with heavy credit card books need to look at where those customers — high-income, digitally active, creditworthy — should be served next. That's likely vehicle loans, travel loans, EV financing, and secured personal lines. Fintechs building credit card alternatives (BNPL, credit lines) face the same regulatory pressure if they grow large enough to attract RBI's attention.

---

## 4. Micro & Small Credit Tripled Its Growth Rate — Something Structural Changed

**The observation:** Micro and Small enterprise credit grew +9.6% YoY in Jan 2025. By Jan 2026, the same metric is +31.2% — a 3x acceleration in 12 months. In absolute terms: ₹7.86L Cr → ₹10.31L Cr, adding ₹2.45L Cr in a single year vs ₹0.69L Cr the year before. Medium enterprises also accelerated: +18.4% (Jan 2025) → +22.3% (Jan 2026). Meanwhile, Large corporate credit slowed to +5.5%.

**Why it matters now:** The 3x acceleration in Micro & Small is too sharp to be purely organic credit demand growth. It coincides with multiple UDYAM-linked policies, PSL target pressures on banks, and possibly a definitional change in what counts as Micro & Small under the revised MSME classification. Some portion is businesses newly visible in bank data for the first time; some is genuine credit expansion. Either way, the direction is clear: small business credit is where the action is in industry lending.

**Implication for lenders:** The lending opportunity in industry has shifted decisively to the smaller end. Large corporate credit grew 12.6% over two years; Micro & Small grew 43.8%. The challenge is underwriting — many of these borrowers have thin or blank bureau files. The edge goes to lenders who can use alternative data (GST filings, UPI history, TReDS invoices) instead of requiring 3 years of ITR and physical collateral.

---

## 5. Services + Personal Loans = 61% of Credit, Both Growing Above Average

**The observation:** Services (₹57.2L Cr) and Personal Loans (₹67.2L Cr) together account for ₹124.5L Cr — 61% of total sectoral bank credit. Both are growing above the system average: Services at +15.5% YoY, Personal Loans at +14.9% YoY (system average: +14.6%). Industry and Agriculture, at 34% of credit combined, are both growing below average (+12.1% and +11.4% respectively).

**Why it matters now:** The credit system has two engines and two laggards. The engines — services and retail — are self-reinforcing: NBFCs (within Services) fund retail segments that banks can't reach, and rising personal credit supports consumption that feeds services revenue. The laggards — industry and agriculture — are not in crisis, but they're losing share year on year. Any lender whose book skews toward industry and agriculture is growing at 11–12% in a 14–15% market.

**Implication for lenders:** The portfolio mix question has a clear answer in this data. Services sub-sectors — NBFCs, Trade, Commercial Real Estate — and personal loan sub-categories — gold, vehicle, education — are where the credit system is adding volume. Teams, products, and credit policies should be calibrated for these segments first.

---

## 6. Engineering Credit Surged 35.9% — A Manufacturing Capex Cycle Has Started

**The observation:** All Engineering grew from ₹1.94L Cr (Jan 2024) to ₹2.56L Cr (Jan 2025) to ₹3.11L Cr (Jan 2026) — +60.5% in 24 months. The YoY acceleration is consistent: +18.0% (Jan 2025), +22.0% (Mar 2025), +35.9% (Jan 2026). This is the highest growth of any industry sub-sector with a balance above ₹1L Cr.

**Why it matters now:** India's Production Linked Incentive (PLI) scheme is creating genuine new manufacturing capacity across electronics, pharmaceuticals, auto components, and specialty chemicals. Companies that qualify for PLI subsidies are investing in new plants, machinery, and supply chains. That capex is being partly funded by bank credit. Three consecutive periods of accelerating growth suggests this is not a one-off project — it's a structural demand increase tied to industrial policy.

**Implication for lenders:** Engineering supply chains offer a well-structured lending opportunity. PLI anchor buyers have large, creditworthy balance sheets and verified supply chains. Their Tier 1 and Tier 2 suppliers need working capital to fulfil orders. These invoices can be verified through GST data and TReDS. The credit risk is lower than unsecured MSME lending at comparable yields because the anchor buyer provides a natural backstop.

---

## 7. PSL Housing: Went From Flat to +37.9% in One Data Point

**The observation:** Priority Sector housing credit was declining or flat for two consecutive periods: -1.3% YoY (Jan 2025), -1.1% YoY (Mar 2025). In Jan 2026 it shows +37.9% YoY, ₹10.31L Cr vs ₹7.47L Cr — an addition of ₹2.84L Cr in 12 months. This is the sharpest acceleration of any PSL sub-category in the dataset and is the opposite direction from where the data was pointing.

**Why it matters now:** A -1% to +38% reversal in 12 months in a ₹10L Cr category is unusual enough to warrant a closer look before accepting it at face value. Three possibilities: (1) PMAY scheme disbursals accelerated due to policy push; (2) banks reclassified existing housing loans as PSL-eligible after a definitional or threshold change; (3) genuine new origination in affordable housing picked up sharply. The implication differs significantly depending on which explanation is correct.

**Implication for lenders:** If the growth is real, the affordable housing opportunity in Tier 3 and 4 cities is very large and confirmed by policy support — PMAY subsidies, small loan sizes, strong repayment behaviour in target areas. If it's partly reclassification, the opportunity is real but smaller. Either way, lenders without distribution in smaller towns are watching this from the sidelines. Business correspondents, co-lending partnerships with HFCs, and agent networks are the access layer that makes this segment reachable.

# Credit Data Gaps — RBI SIBC Jan 2026

> What the data shows on the surface, why it's misleading, and what to do differently.

---

## 1. Food Credit — The January Illusion

**What it shows:** Food credit grew +58.9% YoY in Jan 2026 and +144.4% FY-to-date. The headline bank credit total (₹204.8L Cr) includes this.

**Why it's misleading:** Food credit spikes every January because that's when the government buys kharif crops from farmers through Food Corporation of India. The purchases are funded via bank credit, which inflates the January stock number. By March, it collapses:
- Jan 2024: ₹0.456L Cr → Mar 2024: ₹0.231L Cr (−49%)
- Jan 2025: ₹0.562L Cr → Mar 2025: ₹0.365L Cr (−35%)
- Jan 2026: ₹0.893L Cr → expected Mar 2026: ~₹0.55L Cr

The +144.4% FY growth figure (Jan 2026 vs Mar 2025) is the most misleading number in the entire dataset. It compares the January peak against the March trough.

**What to do differently:** Always use non-food credit as your benchmark. The 14.4% YoY growth in non-food credit is the real signal. Food credit is 0.44% of total bank credit — its +58.9% growth moves the headline by less than 0.1 percentage point. Never quote food credit growth from a January data point without flagging the seasonal distortion.

---

## 2. Agriculture — Which Month You Pick Changes the Story

**What it shows:** Agriculture credit grew +11.4% YoY in Jan 2026 — looks like a steady ~11% grower.

**Why it's misleading:** Agriculture credit is highly seasonal. January captures peak kharif procurement; March captures the post-harvest position. The same sector reads very differently depending on the month:
- Jan 2025: +12.2% YoY
- Mar 2025: +10.4% YoY
- Jan 2026: +11.4% YoY

An 8–10% swing within a single year tells you almost nothing about the underlying trend. The actual underlying trend is 10–12% annual growth, but picking any single data point gives you a misleading precise-looking number.

**What to do differently:** Quote agriculture growth as a range (10–12%) or compare March-to-March only. Services and personal loans are stable across months — use those for precise benchmarking. If a report or competitor quotes agriculture credit growth, check which month the data is from before accepting the number.

---

## 3. Shipping — The Data Broke in Jan 2026

**What it shows:** Shipping credit appears at ₹0.104L Cr in Jan 2026 with a footnote implying +44.5% YoY growth.

**Why it's misleading:** This is a classification break, not real credit data. Shipping credit was completely stable for 4 consecutive periods:
- Jan 2024: ₹7.1L Cr
- Mar 2024: ₹7.1L Cr
- Jan 2025: ₹7.2L Cr
- Mar 2025: ₹7.3L Cr
- Jan 2026: ₹0.104L Cr

A 98.6% drop in one quarter does not happen in a mature ₹7L Cr credit category. The RBI has moved this exposure into a different classification bucket — likely reclassified into infrastructure or a different services sub-category. The ₹7.1L Cr in credit did not disappear; it was relabelled.

**What to do differently:** Exclude Shipping from all trend analysis. The YoY growth figure computed against the reclassified base is meaningless. Wait for RBI to restate the series before drawing any conclusions about shipping finance.

---

## 4. Advances Against Shares and Bonds — Another Reclassification

**What it shows:** "Advances to Individuals against share, bonds, etc." shows small values in Jan 2026 with a growth rate of +5.1% YoY.

**Why it's misleading:** This series showed substantial values in Jan 2024 and Jan 2025 before collapsing to near-zero by Mar 2025. Like Shipping, this is a reclassification event — the exposure was moved elsewhere in the RBI's reporting framework, not repaid. The Jan 2026 value is being compared to an already-reclassified Jan 2025 base, making the +5.1% growth rate meaningless.

**What to do differently:** Exclude this series from personal loans trend analysis. It cannot be used for any portfolio benchmarking until the series is restated. The combined personal loans picture should be read without this line.

---

## 5. Renewable Energy and Social Infrastructure PSL — Series Broke in Mar 2025

**What it shows:** Renewable Energy PSL grew +62% YoY and Social Infrastructure grew +27.7% YoY in Jan 2026.

**Why it's misleading:** Both series had consistent large values through Jan 2025, then dropped 98%+ in Mar 2025:

Renewable Energy:
- Jan 2024: ₹5.4L Cr | Mar 2024: ₹5.8L Cr | Jan 2025: ₹7.6L Cr
- Mar 2025: ₹0.103L Cr | Jan 2026: ₹0.122L Cr

Social Infrastructure shows the same pattern. These are not credit contractions — they are reclassifications of what counts as Renewable Energy and Social Infrastructure under PSL rules. RBI changed the definition or reporting methodology. The YoY growth rates in Jan 2026 are computed against the reclassified tiny base and are nonsense numbers.

**What to do differently:** Do not cite Renewable Energy or Social Infrastructure PSL growth rates from this dataset. The opportunity in renewable energy lending is real, but this data cannot be used to size it. Use project-level data from MNRE or IREDA instead.

---

## 6. MSME Growth — Some of It Is Businesses Becoming Visible, Not New Borrowing

**What it shows:** Micro & Small enterprise credit grew +31.2% YoY in Jan 2026. Medium grew +22.3%. These numbers look like a massive surge in small business borrowing.

**Why it's misleading:** A significant portion of this growth reflects UDYAM-registered businesses entering the formal credit system for the first time — not businesses that previously borrowed and are now borrowing more. GST registration and UDYAM enrolment since 2022 have brought millions of businesses into the formal data. Their credit was always happening (through moneylenders, informal channels, or no credit at all) — it's now being counted for the first time.

The economy didn't grow 31%. The formally-visible part of the MSME economy grew 31%.

**What to do differently:** Treat the growth figure as an upper bound on real credit expansion, not the number itself. More practically: when underwriting new MSME borrowers, expect thin or blank bureau files. These are not bad borrowers — many have strong cash flows. They just have no formal credit history. Lenders who rely on credit scores as the primary underwriting signal will misread this pool entirely.

---

## 7. NBFC Credit Is Not What It Looks Like

**What it shows:** Non-Banking Financial Companies are the largest services sub-sector at ₹19.05L Cr, growing +17.8% YoY. This looks like a massive and growing lending segment.

**Why it's misleading:** This ₹19.05L Cr is bank-to-NBFC lending — wholesale credit lines banks extend to NBFCs. It is not a picture of end borrowers being served. The actual end borrowers (small businesses, microfinance customers, consumer borrowers) are another step downstream, invisible in this data. When you see "NBFCs +17.8%", you are seeing banks funding the NBFC model, not NBFCs directly serving customers.

**What to do differently:** The credit risk for banks in this category is concentrated in a small number of large NBFC counterparties — the top 20 NBFCs probably account for 80%+ of this exposure. It should not be read as diversified retail risk. For NBFCs themselves, this data confirms banks are willing to fund their model at scale — but the credit risk and the customer relationship sit with the NBFC, not the bank.

---

## 8. Priority Sector Numbers Cannot Be Added Up — They Overlap

**What it shows:** Adding up the PSL sub-categories (Agriculture, MSE, Housing, Education, Renewable Energy, etc.) appears to give you the total Priority Sector lending pool.

**Why it's misleading:** PSL categories are not mutually exclusive. The same loan can qualify under multiple categories simultaneously. An agriculture loan to a small farmer may count under Agriculture and Weaker Sections. An MSME loan in an agriculture-adjacent activity may count under both MSE and Agriculture. PSL Housing is a subset of total housing credit — not additional to it.

Adding up all PSL lines will double or triple-count many loans. The resulting sum will be much larger than the actual PSL pool.

**What to do differently:** Only the overall PSL target (40% of net bank credit, i.e., roughly ₹82L Cr of the ₹204.8L Cr total) has legal and analytical meaning. Use sub-category figures only to understand relative growth and composition within PSL — never to estimate the total addressable market.

# Lending Opportunities — RBI SIBC Jan 2026

> Five lending opportunities grounded in the data, each with evidence, who's best positioned, and the real risk.

---

## 1. Gold Loans — Digital Origination, Income-Layered Underwriting

**The opportunity in one sentence:** Gold loan credit is growing at +128.8% YoY and showing no sign of deceleration — the lender who can originate digitally and price by income not just LTV will dominate the next phase of this market.

**Data evidence:**
- Loans against gold jewellery: ₹0.915L Cr (Jan 2024) → ₹1.75L Cr (Jan 2025) → ₹4.01L Cr (Jan 2026)
- YoY growth: +91.4% → +121.1% → +128.8% — the rate is accelerating, not plateauing
- At ₹4.01L Cr, gold loans are still smaller than vehicle loans (₹7.21L Cr) — significant headroom remains
- Gold prices rose ~25–30% over this period; credit grew 4.4x — volume growth, not just price inflation

**Who is best positioned:**
NBFCs with branch networks in Tier 2–4 towns (Muthoot, Manappuram have first-mover depth, but banks and new NBFCs are taking share). The edge in the next phase goes to lenders who can:
1. Do digital gold valuation and reduce branch dependency (cutting cost-to-serve)
2. Overlay income data — GST turnover, UPI inflows, salary credits — to separate lower-risk from stress-driven borrowers within the same LTV band
3. Offer better pricing to income-verified borrowers, attracting the less-stressed cohort

**Key risk:** RBI intervened in 2024 to flag LTV compliance violations at multiple gold lenders. Some of the growth in 2024–25 may include restructured or reclassified loans, not purely new origination. Lenders entering now should be conservative on LTV (60–65% rather than the 75% regulatory maximum) and build income verification into the standard process, not as an exception.

---

## 2. Medium Enterprise Lending — The Fastest-Growing Segment Nobody Is Focused On

**The opportunity in one sentence:** Medium enterprises are growing at +22.3% YoY, hold only 10% of total industry credit, have real GST and banking history, and are too small for large corporate teams but too profitable for microfinance.

**Data evidence:**
- Medium enterprise credit: ₹2.94L Cr (Jan 2024) → ₹3.49L Cr (Jan 2025) → ₹4.26L Cr (Jan 2026)
- Two-year growth: +44.9%; YoY in every period has been above 18% — the most consistent growth rate in industry
- For context, Micro & Small is growing faster (+31.2% YoY) but from a much smaller base and with much thinner credit files
- Large corporate credit grew just 12.6% cumulatively over 2 years — medium is growing 3.5x faster

**Who is best positioned:**
Mid-market-focused NBFCs and private sector banks with dedicated SME teams. The sweet spot is businesses with:
- Annual turnover ₹10–100 Cr
- 2–3 years of GST filings
- Some bureau history (even thin)
- Working capital needs of ₹50L–₹10Cr

These businesses are typically underserved by large corporate banking divisions (too small for the ticket minimum) and overserved with bad products by branches trying to push retail credit. A lender with a dedicated cash flow underwriting model and relationship coverage for this segment has very little competition.

**Key risk:** The 44.9% two-year growth figure includes some MSME formalisation effect — businesses newly classified as Medium under the revised MSME definition after GST registration. Credit quality at the tail of this segment is untested through a full credit cycle. Portfolios need to be screened for businesses that have formalised but don't yet have stable cash flows.

---

## 3. Engineering Supply Chain Finance — PLI-Backed, Digitally Verifiable

**The opportunity in one sentence:** India's PLI scheme is creating ₹3L Cr+ in manufacturing credit demand growing at 35.9% annually — the supply chain of anchor buyers is the lowest-risk entry point.

**Data evidence:**
- All Engineering: ₹1.94L Cr (Jan 2024) → ₹2.56L Cr (Jan 2025) → ₹3.11L Cr (Jan 2026) — +60.5% in 2 years
- YoY acceleration is consistent: +18.0% → +22.0% → +35.9% — not slowing down
- PLI industries (electronics, pharma, auto components, specialty chemicals) all expanding
- Chemicals and Chemical Products: ₹2.42L Cr → ₹3.05L Cr (+25.9% in 2 years), adjacent opportunity

**Who is best positioned:**
Lenders who can structure supply chain finance products (invoice discounting, dealer finance, vendor finance) against PLI-eligible anchor buyers. The ideal product:
1. Lend to Tier 1 and Tier 2 suppliers of PLI anchor buyers (e.g., suppliers to a Tata Electronics or Dixon Technologies)
2. Verify invoices through GST data or TReDS (government's digital invoice discounting platform)
3. Offer working capital at competitive rates backed by the anchor buyer relationship

Banks with existing relationships with PLI anchor buyers are best positioned for the Tier 1 supplier layer. NBFCs with TReDS connectivity can access deeper supply chains. The credit risk is significantly lower than open-market MSME lending because the anchor buyer provides implicit recourse — suppliers won't default on loans tied to orders from their primary customer.

**Key risk:** PLI scheme benefits are conditional on production targets and linked subsidies. If a PLI recipient fails to meet production targets and loses their incentive, they may reduce supplier orders. Lenders should diversify across multiple PLI sectors (not just electronics or just pharma) and assess each anchor buyer's PLI performance track record separately.

---

## 4. Affordable Housing — Tier 3/4 City Distribution Gap

**The opportunity in one sentence:** PSL housing credit surged +37.9% YoY to ₹10.31L Cr with PMAY government support — the constraint is distribution, not demand.

**Data evidence:**
- PSL Housing: ₹8.49L Cr (Jan 2024) → ₹7.47L Cr (Jan 2025, flat/declining) → ₹10.31L Cr (Jan 2026)
- +37.9% YoY — the sharpest acceleration of any PSL category in the dataset
- Regular (non-PSL) housing credit grew just +11.1% YoY — affordable is growing 3.4x faster
- PSL housing qualifies for PMAY subsidies (up to ₹2.67L per borrower in interest subvention for EWS/LIG)

**Who is best positioned:**
Housing Finance Companies (HFCs) with agent networks in Tier 3/4 cities — Aavas Financiers, Home First Finance, Aptus Value Housing — already have the distribution. Banks wanting to grow this category need either:
1. A co-lending structure with one of these HFCs (bank provides funding, HFC provides origination and servicing)
2. A business correspondent network specifically built for semi-urban housing — not the same BCs used for savings or payments

Ticket sizes are typically ₹10–25L. Loss rates in documented self-construction lending to salaried and self-employed borrowers in Tier 3/4 towns are historically low — these borrowers have strong social collateral (community reputation) and are buying/building their primary residence.

**Key risk:** The +37.9% jump from two consecutive flat/declining periods is sharp enough to question whether it's all real origination. Some portion may be reclassification of existing loans. Before allocating capital to PSL housing based on this number, confirm whether the growth is at lenders who already have Tier 3/4 distribution or at banks who are reclassifying urban affordable housing loans.

---

## 5. IT Sector Working Capital — Underleveraged Relative to Revenue

**The opportunity in one sentence:** India's IT sector generates ₹22L Cr+ in annual revenue but holds only ₹0.41L Cr in bank credit — structured working capital products for IT exporters are a white space.

**Data evidence:**
- Computer Software credit: ₹0.263L Cr (Jan 2024) → ₹0.339L Cr (Jan 2025) → ₹0.407L Cr (Jan 2026)
- +54.8% in 2 years, YoY: +28.2% → +27.0% → +20.7%
- At ₹0.41L Cr, bank credit is roughly 0.19% of the IT sector's annual revenue
- Compare: manufacturing sectors with similar revenue have 5–15x higher bank credit ratios

**Who is best positioned:**
Lenders who can underwrite against export invoices and TDS-verified revenue without requiring physical collateral. The product design:
1. Working capital line secured against export receivables (payable by large US/EU corporates — creditworthy counterparties)
2. Tenor: 30–90 days against confirmed purchase orders or invoices
3. Collateral: the export invoice and GST-verified revenue history, not land or machinery
4. Target borrowers: IT services companies with ₹10–500Cr revenue that can't get working capital from their current bank without pledging promoter property

No dominant lender has built this product specifically for IT exporters at scale. The barriers are not regulatory — they are product and sales design. An NBFC or bank with an IT-sector-focused lending team could capture this space before others recognise it.

**Key risk:** IT sector revenue is client-concentration risk. An IT company with 60% revenue from two US clients is a different risk from one with 20+ clients. Underwriting needs to assess client concentration, contract tenure, and whether the export receivable is from a verifiable large-cap counterparty (low risk) or a mid-market US client with its own balance sheet risk (higher risk).

## MERGED GAPS — full series

# Data Gaps — RBI SIBC Merged (Jan 2024 – Feb 2026)

> Period: Jan 2024 – Feb 2026 | Generated: 2026-04-14
> Source: RBI SIBC merged across all ingested periods

---

## 1. January SIBC files do not report Gold Loans or PSL Housing sub-classification

Gold Loans and PSL Housing both appear as null in all January data rows. These series only exist in the March SIBC files. As a result, Jan→Jan YoY comparisons for gold loans and PSL housing are not possible — the cross-period acceleration story for these categories can only be told using March data points.

**Impact:** Any analysis of gold loan trends using merged data must explicitly note that January figures are absent. Readers cannot assess whether the March-to-March trajectory holds in interim months.

---

## 2. January SIBC files do not report NBFC credit or Tourism sub-classification in Services

NBFC credit and Tourism are null in all January service rows. Only March SIBC files capture NBFC sub-classification. Since NBFCs are the largest single services sub-sector, January services totals do not reflect the full picture. The merged Jan→Jan services YoY comparison excludes the NBFC channel.

**Impact:** Services growth rates in January-to-January comparisons understate the true services credit trajectory if NBFC credit grew faster than the other sub-sectors in the year.

---

## 3. Food credit seasonal artifact distorts January bank credit totals

Food credit (NABARD-channelled agricultural procurement credit) peaks in January at kharif marketing season and drops sharply by March. This makes January bank credit totals appear larger than the March figures for the same fiscal year, distorting FY-to-date growth calculations that use January as the base.

**Correct signal:** Non-food credit is the right metric for January-to-January comparisons. Food credit should be stripped from all trend analyses using January data.

---

## 4. PSL Housing +38% FY growth is reclassification, not organic lending

PSL Housing: ₹7.47L Cr (Mar 2025) → ₹10.33L Cr (Feb 2026), +38.4% FY. The Oct 2024 RBI revision raised PSL housing loan ceilings (metro: ₹35L → ₹45L, non-metro: ₹25L → ₹35L). Loans already on bank books that previously exceeded the old limits now qualify as PSL. The ₹2.87L Cr appearing in the data as credit growth is reclassification of existing exposures, not new lending.

**Impact:** PSL Housing growth rates for FY26 are not comparable to prior years. Any lender citing PSL achievement figures for FY26 must disclose the classification change.

---

## 5. PSL totals are not additive across sub-categories

PSL sub-category totals (Housing + Micro & Small + Export Credit + Education + Renewable Energy + Others) do not sum to the PSL aggregate. The discrepancy arises because some loans qualify under multiple PSL categories and are counted in each. Treating sub-category figures as exhaustive slices produces incorrect totals.

**Impact:** Cross-category PSL market share analysis must use category-specific figures, not derived shares from the aggregate.

---

## 6. Consumer Durables YoY deepening contraction is not visible in merged January data alone

Consumer Durables: -1.0% YoY (Jan 2025) → -9.6% YoY (Jan 2026). The worsening trend is only visible with the Jan→Jan comparison enabled by merged data. Any single-period analysis would see a contraction but could not determine whether it was accelerating or stabilizing.

**Impact:** The merged view is essential for distinguishing trend from noise for small, volatile categories like consumer durables. Without it, the policy causation (RBI risk-weight hike driving accelerating contraction) cannot be confirmed.

---

## 7. Other Services and Other Personal Loans remain opaque across all periods

"Other Services" and "Other Personal Loans" are residual catch-all categories. Neither SIBC file provides sub-classification. These residuals are large enough to materially affect sector totals but their composition is unknown across all merged periods.

**Impact:** Any significant change in these residuals cannot be attributed to a structural driver without supplementary data (BSR-1, credit bureau data). They are black boxes in the causal model.

---

## 8. Gems & Jewellery credit conflates gold price appreciation with volume growth

Gems & Jewellery: ₹0.73L Cr (Jan 2025) → ₹0.91L Cr (Jan 2026), +24.7% YoY. Gold prices rose approximately 40% in the same period. If working capital loans are collateral-based and LTV-constrained, a significant portion of the growth is gold price appreciation inflating collateral values and loan amounts — not volume expansion.

**Impact:** Gems & Jewellery YoY growth overstates genuine business expansion during gold price surges. The real growth rate (volume-adjusted) is likely lower.

**The window:** PLI incentive horizons are 5-7 years. Companies approved in 2022-23 are in the investment phase now. The supplier base is expanding. Banks with existing anchor relationships should offer SCF products now, before the anchor companies build their own supply chain financing platforms.

**Action:** Build project finance teams with renewable energy expertise. Develop financing structures for storage-linked solar (which has better bankability than standalone solar). Create a green infrastructure credit desk now — before the drawdown wave begins.
