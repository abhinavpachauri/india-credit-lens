# Lending Opportunities — RBI SIBC Jan 2026

> Five lending opportunities grounded in the data, each with evidence, who's best positioned, and the real risk.

---

## 1. Gold Loans — Digital Origination, Income-Layered Underwriting

**The opportunity in one sentence:** Gold loan credit is growing at +128.8% YoY and showing no sign of deceleration — the lender who can originate digitally and price by income not just LTV will dominate the next phase of this market.

**Data evidence:**
- Loans against gold jewellery: ₹0.915L Cr (Jan 2024) → ₹1.75L Cr (Jan 2025) → ₹4.01L Cr (Jan 2026)
- YoY growth: +91.4% → +121.1% → +128.8% — the rate is accelerating, not plateauing
- At ₹4.01L Cr, gold loans are still smaller than vehicle loans (₹7.21L Cr) — significant headroom remains
- Gold prices rose ~25–30% over this period; credit grew 4.4x — volume growth, not just price inflation

**Who is best positioned:**
NBFCs with branch networks in Tier 2–4 towns (Muthoot, Manappuram have first-mover depth, but banks and new NBFCs are taking share). The edge in the next phase goes to lenders who can:
1. Do digital gold valuation and reduce branch dependency (cutting cost-to-serve)
2. Overlay income data — GST turnover, UPI inflows, salary credits — to separate lower-risk from stress-driven borrowers within the same LTV band
3. Offer better pricing to income-verified borrowers, attracting the less-stressed cohort

**Key risk:** RBI intervened in 2024 to flag LTV compliance violations at multiple gold lenders. Some of the growth in 2024–25 may include restructured or reclassified loans, not purely new origination. Lenders entering now should be conservative on LTV (60–65% rather than the 75% regulatory maximum) and build income verification into the standard process, not as an exception.

## 2. Medium Enterprise Lending — The Fastest-Growing Segment Nobody Is Focused On

**The opportunity in one sentence:** Medium enterprises are growing at +22.3% YoY, hold only 10% of total industry credit, have real GST and banking history, and are too small for large corporate teams but too profitable for microfinance.

**Data evidence:**
- Medium enterprise credit: ₹2.94L Cr (Jan 2024) → ₹3.49L Cr (Jan 2025) → ₹4.26L Cr (Jan 2026)
- Two-year growth: +44.9%; YoY in every period has been above 18% — the most consistent growth rate in industry
- For context, Micro & Small is growing faster (+31.2% YoY) but from a much smaller base and with much thinner credit files
- Large corporate credit grew just 12.6% cumulatively over 2 years — medium is growing 3.5x faster

**Who is best positioned:**
Mid-market-focused NBFCs and private sector banks with dedicated SME teams. The sweet spot is businesses with:
- Annual turnover ₹10–100 Cr
- 2–3 years of GST filings
- Some bureau history (even thin)
- Working capital needs of ₹50L–₹10Cr

These businesses are typically underserved by large corporate banking divisions (too small for the ticket minimum) and overserved with bad products by branches trying to push retail credit. A lender with a dedicated cash flow underwriting model and relationship coverage for this segment has very little competition.

**Key risk:** The 44.9% two-year growth figure includes some MSME formalisation effect — businesses newly classified as Medium under the revised MSME definition after GST registration. Credit quality at the tail of this segment is untested through a full credit cycle. Portfolios need to be screened for businesses that have formalised but don't yet have stable cash flows.

## 3. Engineering Supply Chain Finance — PLI-Backed, Digitally Verifiable

**The opportunity in one sentence:** India's PLI scheme is creating ₹3L Cr+ in manufacturing credit demand growing at 35.9% annually — the supply chain of anchor buyers is the lowest-risk entry point.

**Data evidence:**
- All Engineering: ₹1.94L Cr (Jan 2024) → ₹2.56L Cr (Jan 2025) → ₹3.11L Cr (Jan 2026) — +60.5% in 2 years
- YoY acceleration is consistent: +18.0% → +22.0% → +35.9% — not slowing down
- PLI industries (electronics, pharma, auto components, specialty chemicals) all expanding
- Chemicals and Chemical Products: ₹2.42L Cr → ₹3.05L Cr (+25.9% in 2 years), adjacent opportunity

**Who is best positioned:**
Lenders who can structure supply chain finance products (invoice discounting, dealer finance, vendor finance) against PLI-eligible anchor buyers. The ideal product:
1. Lend to Tier 1 and Tier 2 suppliers of PLI anchor buyers (e.g., suppliers to a Tata Electronics or Dixon Technologies)
2. Verify invoices through GST data or TReDS (government's digital invoice discounting platform)
3. Offer working capital at competitive rates backed by the anchor buyer relationship

Banks with existing relationships with PLI anchor buyers are best positioned for the Tier 1 supplier layer. NBFCs with TReDS connectivity can access deeper supply chains. The credit risk is significantly lower than open-market MSME lending because the anchor buyer provides implicit recourse — suppliers won't default on loans tied to orders from their primary customer.

**Key risk:** PLI scheme benefits are conditional on production targets and linked subsidies. If a PLI recipient fails to meet production targets and loses their incentive, they may reduce supplier orders. Lenders should diversify across multiple PLI sectors (not just electronics or just pharma) and assess each anchor buyer's PLI performance track record separately.

## 4. Affordable Housing — Tier 3/4 City Distribution Gap

**The opportunity in one sentence:** PSL housing credit surged +37.9% YoY to ₹10.31L Cr with PMAY government support — the constraint is distribution, not demand.

**Data evidence:**
- PSL Housing: ₹8.49L Cr (Jan 2024) → ₹7.47L Cr (Jan 2025, flat/declining) → ₹10.31L Cr (Jan 2026)
- +37.9% YoY — the sharpest acceleration of any PSL category in the dataset
- Regular (non-PSL) housing credit grew just +11.1% YoY — affordable is growing 3.4x faster
- PSL housing qualifies for PMAY subsidies (up to ₹2.67L per borrower in interest subvention for EWS/LIG)

**Who is best positioned:**
Housing Finance Companies (HFCs) with agent networks in Tier 3/4 cities — Aavas Financiers, Home First Finance, Aptus Value Housing — already have the distribution. Banks wanting to grow this category need either:
1. A co-lending structure with one of these HFCs (bank provides funding, HFC provides origination and servicing)
2. A business correspondent network specifically built for semi-urban housing — not the same BCs used for savings or payments

Ticket sizes are typically ₹10–25L. Loss rates in documented self-construction lending to salaried and self-employed borrowers in Tier 3/4 towns are historically low — these borrowers have strong social collateral (community reputation) and are buying/building their primary residence.

**Key risk:** The +37.9% jump from two consecutive flat/declining periods is sharp enough to question whether it's all real origination. Some portion may be reclassification of existing loans. Before allocating capital to PSL housing based on this number, confirm whether the growth is at lenders who already have Tier 3/4 distribution or at banks who are reclassifying urban affordable housing loans.

## 5. IT Sector Working Capital — Underleveraged Relative to Revenue

**The opportunity in one sentence:** India's IT sector generates ₹22L Cr+ in annual revenue but holds only ₹0.41L Cr in bank credit — structured working capital products for IT exporters are a white space.

**Data evidence:**
- Computer Software credit: ₹0.263L Cr (Jan 2024) → ₹0.339L Cr (Jan 2025) → ₹0.407L Cr (Jan 2026)
- +54.8% in 2 years, YoY: +28.2% → +27.0% → +20.7%
- At ₹0.41L Cr, bank credit is roughly 0.19% of the IT sector's annual revenue
- Compare: manufacturing sectors with similar revenue have 5–15x higher bank credit ratios

**Who is best positioned:**
Lenders who can underwrite against export invoices and TDS-verified revenue without requiring physical collateral. The product design:
1. Working capital line secured against export receivables (payable by large US/EU corporates — creditworthy counterparties)
2. Tenor: 30–90 days against confirmed purchase orders or invoices
3. Collateral: the export invoice and GST-verified revenue history, not land or machinery
4. Target borrowers: IT services companies with ₹10–500Cr revenue that can't get working capital from their current bank without pledging promoter property

No dominant lender has built this product specifically for IT exporters at scale. The barriers are not regulatory — they are product and sales design. An NBFC or bank with an IT-sector-focused lending team could capture this space before others recognise it.

**Key risk:** IT sector revenue is client-concentration risk. An IT company with 60% revenue from two US clients is a different risk from one with 20+ clients. Underwriting needs to assess client concentration, contract tenure, and whether the export receivable is from a verifiable large-cap counterparty (low risk) or a mid-market US client with its own balance sheet risk (higher risk).
