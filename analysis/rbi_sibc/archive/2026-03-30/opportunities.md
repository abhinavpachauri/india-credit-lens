## 1. Gold Lending — Banks are now in the game

**The opportunity in one sentence:** Gold loans grew from ₹0.93L Cr to ₹4.29L Cr in 24 months; banks are taking market share from gold NBFCs and the market is large enough to support both.

**Data evidence:**
- Gold Loans (personalLoans): ₹0.93L Cr (Mar 2024) → ₹2.06L Cr (Mar 2025, +121.1% YoY) → ₹4.29L Cr (Feb 2026, +107.8% FY)
- Gold as % of Personal Loans: 1.75% → 3.45% → 6.31%
- Total bank gold lending has grown faster than Muthoot + Manappuram combined AUM, signalling genuine market share capture
- Gems and Jewellery industrial credit (industryByType): +35.8% FY, confirming gold price-driven demand at the commercial level too

**Who is best positioned and why:**
Public sector banks and mid-size private banks with strong branch networks in tier-2/3 cities. Gold lending is operationally intensive (assay, cash management, vault) — scale and branch density are the moat. Fintech-only approaches haven't worked; this is a physical game.

**Key risk or caveat:**
The RBI has increased scrutiny on gold loan LTV ratios and auction processes. Banks entering late may do so into a stricter regulatory environment. Gold price retracement would compress collateral values and require re-processing of thousands of loans. Build in 15% gold price decline stress tests from day one.

## 2. MSME Credit — The formalisation wave is bankable right now

**The opportunity in one sentence:** MSME industrial credit growing +29.2% FY represents the largest addressable incremental credit market in the Indian banking system at this moment.

**Data evidence:**
- Micro and Small (industryBySize): ₹7.98L Cr (Mar 2025) → ₹10.32L Cr (Feb 2026), +29.2% FY
- Medium (industryBySize): ₹3.63L Cr → ₹4.30L Cr, +18.4% FY
- PSL Micro and Small Enterprises (prioritySector): ₹22.4L Cr → ₹28.1L Cr, +25.6% FY
- Net addition of ₹2.34L Cr to Micro and Small in 11 months — more than Large corporates added (₹1.61L Cr)

**Who is best positioned and why:**
Lenders with alternative underwriting capabilities — GST return analysis, bank statement cash-flow lending, UDYAM-linked scoring, UPI transaction history. Traditional P&L-based underwriting doesn't work for MSMEs with low declared incomes. The first cohort of digitally-onboarded GST-registered MSMEs (2019-22 registrations) now has 3-4 years of formal financial history — enough for a repayment track record.

**Key risk or caveat:**
Some of the 29.2% growth reflects reclassification (MSME definition boundaries shifted under MSMED Act amendments) rather than net new lending. Stress test portfolios against the scenario where 20-25% of "new" MSME accounts are actually reclassified from large/informal categories — adjust expected default rates accordingly.

## 3. PLI Manufacturing Supply Chain Finance

**The opportunity in one sentence:** All Engineering credit +36% YoY creates a supply chain finance opportunity for anchor-based origination at scale.

**Data evidence:**
- All Engineering (industryByType): ₹1.96L Cr (Feb 2024) → ₹2.32L Cr (Feb 2025) → ₹3.16L Cr (Feb 2026), +36.0% YoY
- Chemicals (industryByType): ₹2.68L Cr → ₹3.13L Cr, +17.0% FY
- Petroleum (industryByType): ₹1.54L Cr → ₹1.85L Cr, +20.3% FY
- Total manufacturing capex credit (Engineering + Chemicals + Petroleum) growing well above the 11.6% industry average

**Who is best positioned and why:**
Banks that signed on PLI scheme anchor companies (Samsung, Tata Electronics, Dixon, BHEL) as current account / working capital clients during 2022-24. Anchor-buyer supply chain finance programs let the anchor's credit rating extend to its 50-100 suppliers without requiring individual MSME underwriting. One relationship generates a portfolio of smaller counterparties with anchor-guaranteed invoice validation.

**Key risk or caveat:**
PLI schemes have production-linked disbursement milestones. If a beneficiary misses targets, their incentive entitlement — and sometimes their willingness to draw down — changes. Credit exposure to PLI companies should include a monitoring trigger for incentive disbursement status.

## 4. Renewable Energy Project Finance — A decade-long gap

**The opportunity in one sentence:** India needs to deploy ₹20-25L Cr in renewable energy investment by 2030; bank credit stands at ₹0.14L Cr — the largest gap between need and current credit supply in the entire dataset.

**Data evidence:**
- Renewable Energy PSL (prioritySector): ₹0.06L Cr (Mar 2024) → ₹0.10L Cr (Mar 2025, +78.3%) → ₹0.14L Cr (Feb 2026, +35.2% FY)
- Growing consistently but starting from near-zero base
- As % of total bank credit: 0.007% — functionally invisible
- India's 2030 target: 500 GW renewable capacity; current installation pace requires massive finance scaling

**Who is best positioned and why:**
Banks that invest now in renewable energy credit underwriting capabilities: solar irradiance risk models, offtake agreement creditworthiness assessment (DISCOM credit quality is the key variable), battery storage residual value models, grid connectivity risk in new infrastructure states. There is no incumbent with a structural edge — the first 3-5 banks to build the capability establish it.

**Key risk or caveat:**
The primary risk is DISCOM (power distribution company) creditworthiness — if the offtaker can't pay, the project can't service debt. State DISCOM financial health varies widely. Prioritise projects in states with payment security mechanisms (Rajasthan, Karnataka) and avoid highly stressed DISCOMs. Regulatory change risk on wheeling charges is secondary but real.

## 5. Vehicle Finance — EV-specific credit is 2-3 years ahead of market standards

**The opportunity in one sentence:** Vehicle loans +16.6% FY, and EV adoption is creating a product gap that no Indian lender has filled: credit designed for the EV risk profile.

**Data evidence:**
- Vehicle Loans (personalLoans): ₹6.23L Cr (Mar 2025) → ₹7.26L Cr (Feb 2026), +16.6% FY
- Up from +8.6% YoY in FY25 — growth accelerating
- India's EV sales crossed 20L units in FY25; EV penetration in 2-wheelers is now ~5%, 3-wheelers ~55%, 4-wheelers ~2-3%
- Vehicles, Vehicle Parts and Transport Equipment (industryByType): +14.8% FY — commercial vehicle and component manufacturing also growing

**Who is best positioned and why:**
Lenders who build specific EV credit products: battery health monitoring integration (via OBD or OEM data feed), green EMI with subsidy-linked monthly payments (FAME, state subsidies), residual value guarantees linked to battery state of health rather than generic depreciation tables. Fleet EV lending (e-commerce, last-mile logistics) is the highest-volume entry point — fleets have higher credit quality than individual borrowers and need larger ticket sizes.

**Key risk or caveat:**
ICE vehicle resale market is well-established; EV residual values have high uncertainty, especially as technology evolves rapidly. Lenders who anchor EMI structures on resale value guarantees without battery degradation risk management will face surprises at loan maturity. Partner with OEMs on buy-back programs before building large EV books.
