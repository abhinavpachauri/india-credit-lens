# Lending Opportunities — RBI SIBC Mar 2026 (annual snapshot, published Apr 30 2026)

Five highest-conviction opportunities visible in the FY26 data. Each is
grounded in absolute flow numbers, not generic theme-spotting. Conviction is
ranked by combined velocity and size of the underlying wallet.

---

## 1. Co-lending and warehouse-financing infrastructure for the bank-NBFC cycle

**The opportunity:** With bank credit to NBFCs growing **26.3% YoY** and
incremental flow of **₹4.30L Cr in FY26 alone**, the bilateral co-lending and
structured warehouse arrangements between banks and NBFCs are the most active
product wedge in the system. Banks need NBFC origination reach; NBFCs need
balance sheet capacity.

**Data evidence:** NBFCs (Services line) at ₹20.66L Cr (Mar 2026), up from
₹15.23L Cr (Mar 2024). FY25 added ₹1.12L Cr; FY26 added ₹4.30L Cr — nearly 4x
the prior-year flow.

**Best-positioned lender:** Private banks with strong NBFC relationships (Axis,
ICICI, HDFC, IndusInd, Federal). For tech and product builders: loan-management
systems with co-lending support, partition logic, and audit-trail rigour have a
12-18 month buying window before in-house tooling at large banks fills the gap.

**Key risk / caveat:** RBI could re-tighten risk weights again if it perceives
NBFC sector overheating. Watch H1 FY27 RBI Financial Stability Reports closely.

---

## 2. Alternative-data underwriting stack for thin-file MSMEs

**The opportunity:** Micro and Small industrial credit grew **33.1% YoY** in
FY26 — the largest single acceleration in the file. Absolute add was
**₹2.65L Cr in FY26 alone**, four times the FY25 flow. A meaningful share of
this is first-cycle formal borrowers (post-GST, post-UDYAM) with thin or blank
bureau histories. Bureau-only underwriting cannot scale to this book.

**Data evidence:** Industry-by-size view: Micro and Small at ₹10.63L Cr (Mar
2026), up from ₹7.98L Cr (Mar 2025). PSL MSE confirms the trajectory:
₹29.0L Cr at Mar 2026, +29.5% YoY.

**Best-positioned lender:** MSME-focused NBFCs (Bajaj Finance, Cholamandalam,
Aditya Birla Capital), small-finance banks (Equitas, Suryoday, Bandhan), and
B2B fintechs (Indifi, Lendingkart, Capital Float). For tech builders: data
partnerships with GSPs and account aggregators are the leverage point — alt-data
underwriting models trained on GST, UPI, and e-way-bill signals.

**Key risk / caveat:** First-cycle MSME borrowers have no through-the-cycle
performance history. FY27-28 will be the first stress test for alternative-data
underwriting models. Loss curves could surprise.

## 3. Specialised gold-loan operations at scale

**The opportunity:** Gold Loans grew 5x in two years to ₹4.60L Cr — the most
dramatic personal-credit reclassification in recent memory. Banks struggle
operationally with the physical-collateral handling, valuation, and short-tenor
cadence that gold loans require. Specialised NBFCs and tier-3/4 banks own the
disbursement velocity.

**Data evidence:** Gold Loans at ₹4.60L Cr (Mar 2026), up from ₹2.06L Cr (Mar
2025) and ₹0.93L Cr (Mar 2024). Two consecutive YoY rates above 120%. Gold
share of personal credit: 1.7% → 6.6% in 24 months.

**Best-positioned lender:** Pure-play gold NBFCs (Manappuram, Muthoot, IIFL
Finance) and southern banks with branch density (Federal Bank, KVB, South
Indian Bank, Karnataka Bank). For new entrants: partnership models with gold
NBFCs are operationally faster than building branch operations from zero.

**Key risk / caveat:** A material gold-price reversal (>20% decline) would
trigger collateral re-valuation across the book. LTV ratios on the marginal
loan are already aggressive; stock effect from past reclassifications would
amplify reported book contraction in a downturn scenario.

## 4. Vehicle-supply-chain financing for the EV and Tier-2 component build-out

**The opportunity:** Two parallel signals point at the same theme. Vehicle
Loans (personal) accelerated from 8.6% to **18.6% YoY** in FY26 — over 2x the
absolute pace. Vehicles, Vehicle Parts and Transport Equipment industrial
credit jumped from 5.2% to **18.1% YoY**. The OEM EV ramp-up (Tata, Mahindra,
Hyundai-India) is now propagating into Tier-1 and Tier-2 component capex.

**Data evidence:** Vehicle Loans (personal): ₹7.39L Cr at Mar 2026, +18.6%.
Vehicles & Parts (industrial): ₹1.41L Cr at Mar 2026, +18.1% YoY. FY26 incremental
on personal vehicle loans alone was ₹1.16L Cr.

**Best-positioned lender:** OEM captive NBFCs (Bajaj Finance, M&M Finance, Tata
Motors Finance) on the personal side. On the supply-chain side: TReDS platforms
(M1xchange, Invoicemart, RXIL), bilateral supply-chain finance teams at private
banks, and specialist NBFCs (Mahindra Finance Vendor, Cholamandalam SCF).

**Key risk / caveat:** EV adoption rates lagging policy targets could compress
Tier-1 component capex pipelines. Watch Q2 FY27 PV and 2W EV penetration data
closely.

## 5. Capex term-loan books for industrial revival across Petroleum, Engineering, and Metals

**The opportunity:** The PLI capex story has spread well beyond electronics in
FY26. All Engineering grew 32.2%, Petroleum 32.5%, Basic Metal 19.4%, Cement
turned from -0.0% to +9.0%, Vehicles & Parts to 18.1%. PSU banks
underweight industrial term loans have under-priced upside; private banks
likely already over-allocated.

**Data evidence (FY26 YoY):**
- All Engineering: 22.0 → 32.2% (₹3.17L Cr)
- Petroleum, Coal: 16.5 → 32.5% (₹2.04L Cr)
- Basic Metal: 12.8 → 19.4% (₹5.18L Cr)
- Cement: -0.0 → 9.0% (₹0.78L Cr)
- Chemicals: 7.4 → 14.9% (₹3.08L Cr)
- Food Processing: 5.1 → 14.0% (₹2.50L Cr)

**Best-positioned lender:** PSU banks with project-finance teams (SBI, Bank of
Baroda, Canara) for large-ticket term loans. Private banks (Axis, ICICI, Kotak)
for working-capital tie-ins to capex projects. Specialised infrastructure
financiers (REC, PFC, IIFCL) for clean-tech and renewable-adjacent capex.

**Key risk / caveat:** Industrial credit has historically been the most
cyclical book. A consumption slowdown in H2 FY27 would hit working capital
draws first. Staircase-based EAD modelling and sensitivity analysis around
demand assumptions are essential.
