# Credit Insights — RBI SIBC Feb 2026

> Period: Feb 2026 | Publication date: 2026-03-30
> Source: RBI Sector and Industry-wise Bank Credit (SIBC30032026.xlsx)
> Data: 3 absolute dates — Mar 2024, Mar 2025, Feb 2026

---

## 1. Bank credit is growing faster in FY26 than all of FY25

Bank credit reached ₹207.5L Cr in Feb 2026, up 13.8% FY (vs Mar 2025). The full FY25 YoY was 11.0%. With one month left in FY26, the system is already on a stronger trajectory than the prior year. The absolute addition from Mar 2025 to Feb 2026 is ₹25.1L Cr — compared to ₹18.1L Cr added in all of FY25.

**Why it matters now:** This isn't base effect or a single month spike. Eleven months of data on the same trajectory confirms that FY26 is a structural acceleration year, not a recovery from a weak prior year. The credit cycle is in expansion.

**Implication for lenders:** Any portfolio that grew less than 13% in FY26 (to Feb) lost market share.

---

## 2. Gold loans tripled in 24 months — this is the biggest personal credit story

Gold loans: ₹0.93L Cr (Mar 2024) → ₹2.06L Cr (Mar 2025) → ₹4.29L Cr (Feb 2026). That is +359% in 24 months. Share of personal loans: 1.75% → 3.45% → 6.31%. This is not a product cycle — it is a structural shift in how Indian households borrow. Gold-backed credit is replacing unsecured personal loans as the preferred short-tenure borrowing instrument.

**Why it matters now:** The RBI tightened unsecured credit (credit cards, consumer durables) in 2023-24. Gold lending is the unconstrained alternative. Households are responding to price signals.

**Implication for lenders:** Banks entering gold lending now are taking share from NBFCs. The product moat is operational (branch-level assay, cash management) not regulatory. Move fast.

---

## 3. Micro and Small enterprise credit accelerated to +29.2% FY — 5× faster than Large

Micro and Small grew from ₹7.98L Cr (Mar 2025) to ₹10.32L Cr (Feb 2026). Large enterprises grew only +5.7% FY. This inversion — where the smallest borrowers are growing fastest — is confirmed by PSL Micro and Small (+25.6% FY). Two independent series pointing in the same direction.

The driver is structural: UDYAM registration has put millions of MSMEs into the formal financial system. GST data has made them underwritable. Banks have had 3 years to build MSME origination infrastructure — the ones who did are now harvesting.

**Implication for lenders:** The next ₹10L Cr of industrial credit will come from SMEs. If your industrial book is predominantly large corporate, your growth rate is structurally capped.

---

## 4. PSL Housing anomaly — this is a classification event, not an organic surge

PSL Housing: ₹7.55L Cr (Mar 2024) → ₹7.47L Cr (Mar 2025, -1.1%) → ₹10.33L Cr (Feb 2026, +38.4% FY). A near-doubling in 11 months after a year of contraction. The only credible explanation is the Oct 2024 RBI revision raising PSL housing loan limits: metro cities from ₹35L to ₹45L, non-metro from ₹25L to ₹35L.

Loans already on bank books that previously exceeded the limit now qualify. This is reclassification, not new lending. The +₹2.87L Cr appears in the data as credit growth but represents the same underlying exposures.

**Implication for lenders:** Never quote PSL housing growth without this context. The genuine new lending component is far smaller. Calibrate accordingly when reporting PSL achievement.

---

## 5. All Engineering credit +36% YoY — PLI manufacturing is now a banking reality

All Engineering: ₹1.96L Cr (Feb 2024) → ₹2.32L Cr (Feb 2025) → ₹3.16L Cr (Feb 2026). +62% in 2 years. This is the fastest-growing large industry sub-sector in the data. Production-Linked Incentive schemes (electronics, defence, medical devices, EV components) have crossed the announcement phase and are now driving real capex credit drawdowns.

**Why it matters now:** PLI schemes have 5-7 year incentive horizons. Companies that received approvals in 2022-23 are now in the investment phase. The credit wave is just starting.

**Implication for lenders:** Banks that built PLI-sector relationships during application phases are seeing drawdowns now. Supply chain finance to tier-2 and tier-3 PLI suppliers is the next layer — the anchor provides implicit credit assurance.

---

## 6. NBFC borrowing from banks re-accelerating at +19.1% FY after regulatory tightening

Bank credit to NBFCs: ₹16.35L Cr (Mar 2025) → ₹19.48L Cr (Feb 2026). Adding ₹3.12L Cr in 11 months. After the 2023-24 risk weight increases on NBFC credit, which temporarily slowed flows, the channel is fully open again. NBFCs are the fastest-growing services sub-sector.

This matters because NBFCs are intermediaries — this ₹3.12L Cr flows onward to gold borrowers, MSMEs, vehicle buyers, and personal loan customers. The NBFC re-acceleration amplifies every other lending category.

**Implication for lenders:** Co-origination and BC partnerships with NBFCs are back in full play. Banks that pulled back from NBFC relationships in FY24 may now find margin terms have moved against them.

---

## 7. Credit cards, consumer durables — two categories stalling simultaneously

Credit Card Outstanding: +2.7% FY in FY26, down from +10.6% YoY in FY25. Consumer Durables: -5.5% FY, -1.0% YoY in FY25. Two consecutive periods of contraction for durables. Two consecutive periods of deceleration for cards.

This is not coincidence — these are both unsecured, high-risk categories targeted by the same RBI policy action. Risk weights on credit card receivables and tighter underwriting norms have had their intended effect.

**Implication for lenders:** BNPL, UPI credit lines, and sachet personal loans are absorbing the displaced demand. The credit is not disappearing — it is migrating to untracked channels. Traditional credit card issuers should invest in UPI credit-linked products before this migration completes.

---

## 8. Infrastructure credit decelerating — the FY22-24 supercycle is over

Infrastructure: +5.8% FY in FY26, down from high single-digit/low double-digit growth in FY22-24. At ₹14.44L Cr (32.5% of all industry credit), infrastructure's growth rate matters systemically. Projects built during the 2019-24 capex wave are now in operations — drawing down loans, not drawing new ones.

The next capex wave (data centers, green hydrogen, semiconductor fab) has not yet reached credit drawdown scale.

**Implication for lenders:** Infrastructure credit books built on the last cycle will have high repayments and low new origination. Corporate banks with heavy infra exposure need to start building the credit capability for the next cycle.
