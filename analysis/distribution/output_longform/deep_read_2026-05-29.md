# The deep read, May 2026: what credit and payments data say when you read them together

The release notes cover what each dataset says on its own. This one is different: it reads the credit data and the payments data as one system — where they agree, where they disagree, and what that means for lending. The disagreements are usually the most useful part.


## The cross-system reads


**Cross-system signal: credit_card flow softening ahead of stock**

Credit card spending is slowing down, which usually happens before credit card loans start shrinking. When people spend less on their cards, they typically pay down their balances over the next few months, causing total credit card debt to drop. This gives banks a few months' head start to see what's coming.

> So what: Banks should prepare for lower credit card loan growth and adjust their lending plans before the slowdown hits their loan books.


**Unsecured retail credit appetite — expanding (5/5 measurements observed)**

Personal loans for credit cards and other purposes grew 3.78 percent and 12.7 percent year-on-year, while credit card balances outstanding rose 8.15 percent. Consumer durable loans fell 3.38 percent. Most unsecured lending is expanding, meaning banks are willing to lend more to people without collateral because they see demand and acceptable risk.

> So what: A bank should grow its unsecured personal loan book now, especially in the other personal loan category growing at 12.7 percent, while watching credit quality closely since these loans have no asset backing.

How this read is computed:

- Unsecured retail credit appetite → expanding
- Credit Card Outstanding (Credit): up, +3.8% YoY
- Other Personal Loans (Credit): up, +12.7% YoY
- Consumer Durables (Credit): up, -3.4% YoY
- Credit card spend (value) (Payments): down, falling
- Credit cards outstanding (Payments): up, +8.2% YoY

**Card-based consumption activity — expanding (5/5 measurements observed)**

Card terminals grew 1.45 percent, vehicle loans jumped 17.97 percent, but consumer durable loans fell 3.38 percent. People are borrowing heavily to buy cars and trucks, using cards a bit more, yet pulling back on financing fridges and TVs. The pattern says households are spending selectively—big-ticket transport yes, home goods no.

> So what: A bank should push auto finance hard, keep card offers steady, and avoid chasing consumer-durable portfolios until that 3.38 percent decline reverses.

How this read is computed:

- Card-based consumption activity → expanding
- Credit card spend (value) (Payments): down, falling
- Debit card spend (value) (Payments): down, falling
- POS terminals (Payments): up, +1.4% YoY
- Consumer Durables (Credit): up, -3.4% YoY
- Vehicle Loans (Credit): up, +18.0% YoY

**Unsecured spend–stock cycle — partially engaged (3/3 segments live)**

This is a feedback cycle read across both pipelines: spending, balances, and appetite feed each other. The basis below shows each segment and whether it is currently running with the cycle or against it.

> So what: A fully-running cycle compounds; a segment moving against it is the early warning that the cycle is turning. Watch the reversed segment.

How this read is computed:

- Unsecured spend–stock cycle → partial
- Unsecured retail credit appetite → Credit card spend (value): up, running

## Live openings — Credit


**Co-lending & warehouse-financing infrastructure**

Small business credit is growing at 30.09 percent this year and now makes up 23.12 percent of all business lending. Growth in micro and small business lending jumped by 23.77 percentage points, driven by government systems like UDYAM registration and GST data that make it easier to check if tiny businesses are real and creditworthy. Banks cannot reach thousands of small borrowers alone, so they need partners who can do the ground work of finding customers and verifying them.

> So what: The bank should build partnerships with fintech companies and NBFCs that specialize in reaching small businesses, using these partners to handle customer acquisition and initial checks while the bank provides the funding.


**Gold loan market entry / deepening for banks**

Banks are growing gold loans by 121.21 percent compared to last year, and this growth has been climbing for 10.0 periods in a row. Gold prices jumped sharply between 2023 and 2026, so customers can now borrow much more money using the same gold jewelry or coins as security. Banks are winning customers from finance companies because they have local branches where staff can check the gold is real and store it safely, plus they charge lower interest rates.

> So what: Banks should open more gold loan counters in branches, train staff to test gold quickly, and advertise lower rates to pull customers from finance companies while gold prices stay high.


**MSME origination infrastructure investment window**

Small business lending is growing at 30.09 pct per year, and these tiny businesses now make up 23.12 pct of all MSME lending. Their growth rate jumped by 23.77 pp recently, reaching 32.68 pct, while bigger MSME borrowers are growing 20.06 pp slower. Banks can now check if these small businesses can repay loans using government registration systems and GST records, which is why lending to them is taking off.

> So what: A bank should invest in systems that pull data from UDYAM registration and GST records to approve small business loans quickly, because these borrowers are growing much faster than larger ones.


## Live openings — Payments


**Credit card penetration deepening — PSB share gap is the opening**

India has 119436129.0 credit cards growing at 8.15 percent per year. Private banks own 71.01 percent of all cards, while public sector banks hold only 23.8 percent. Public sector banks already have millions of debit card customers who trust them and know them, but those customers don't have credit cards yet.

> So what: Public sector banks should offer credit cards to their existing debit card customers, because they already have the relationship and the customer data to lend safely.


**Rural cash distribution via Micro ATM / Business Correspondent networks**

There are 1367989.0 micro ATMs in rural India today, down -7.17 percent from last year. These machines are the cheapest way to get cash to remote villages where building full branches or ATMs costs too much. Fewer machines means fewer people can get cash easily.

> So what: The bank should invest in more micro ATMs or partner with business correspondents to expand rural cash access before competitors fill the gap.


**Embedded transit and tap-to-pay payment products**

Credit card spending in the 'other' category jumped 492.78% in value and 299.83% in volume this year. People are tapping their cards to pay for metro rides, bus tickets, and quick store purchases—things they used to pay cash or debit for before. New tap-to-pay systems at metro gates and shop counters turned credit cards into an everyday payment tool.

> So what: The bank should issue more credit cards and partner with transit systems and small merchants to install tap-to-pay terminals, because customers now want to use credit cards for daily small purchases.


## Watch-outs


**Infrastructure repayment wave with insufficient new origination pipeline**

Banks over-indexed to traditional infrastructure credit face a structural squeeze: the FY22-24 project book moves to repayment (compressing balances) while the next-cycle pipeline (renewables, data centres) is slower to ramp. Banks without next-cycle project finance capability face credit portfolio contraction.

> So what: Move on this while the trend is still running in your favour.


**CIC reporting compliance gaps creating off-balance-sheet credit risk**

RBI Digital Lending Guidelines (2022/2025) require BNPL and digital credit to report to Credit Information Companies. However, compliance may be incomplete among newer digital lenders operating under first-loss guarantee structures or co-lending arrangements where the regulated entity's on-book exposure is partially shielded. If reporting gaps exist, the true unsecured credit stress is understated in bank balance sheets and in SIBC data.

> So what: Move on this while the trend is still running in your favour.


**Credit card market concentrated in top-5 private banks**

The top-5 private banks control approximately 71% of credit card outstanding. The largest single issuer holds ~20% of the market. This concentration creates systemic fragility — as demonstrated when a single payment bank's exit caused a market-wide debit card step-down. Similar disruption from a major credit card issuer would have proportionally larger effects.

> So what: Move on this while the trend is still running in your favour.
