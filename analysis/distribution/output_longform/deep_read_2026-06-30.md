# The deep read, May 2026: what credit and payments data say when you read them together

The release notes cover what each dataset says on its own. This one is different: it reads the credit data and the payments data as one system — where they agree, where they disagree, and what that means for lending. The disagreements are usually the most useful part.

## The cross-system reads


**Cross-system signal: credit_card flow leading stock — origination headroom**

Credit card spending at shops (POS) is up 9.32 percent and online (e-commerce) is up 4.97 percent, but the actual credit card loans outstanding grew only 1.33 percent. ATM cash withdrawals on cards fell 1.0 percent. People are swiping cards more but not borrowing much more yet, which means there is room to lend.

> So what: The bank should push credit card loans now because customers are spending but loan balances have not caught up, so there is clear headroom to grow the book before demand cools.

**Unsecured retail credit appetite — expanding (5/5 measurements observed)**

Personal loans for credit cards and other purposes grew 3.78 percent and 12.7 percent year-on-year, while credit card balances outstanding rose 8.15 percent. Consumer durable loans fell 3.38 percent. Most unsecured lending is expanding, meaning banks are willing to lend more to people without collateral because they see demand and acceptable risk.

> So what: A bank should grow its unsecured personal loan book now, especially in the other personal loan category growing at 12.7 percent, while watching credit quality closely since these loans have no asset backing.

**Card-based consumption activity — expanding (5/5 measurements observed)**

Card terminals grew 1.45 percent, vehicle loans jumped 17.97 percent, but consumer durable loans fell 3.38 percent. People are borrowing heavily to buy cars and trucks, using cards a bit more, yet pulling back on financing fridges and TVs. The pattern says households are spending selectively—big-ticket transport yes, home goods no.

> So what: A bank should push auto finance hard, keep card offers steady, and avoid chasing consumer-durable portfolios until that 3.38 percent decline reverses.

## Live openings — Credit


**Co-lending & warehouse-financing infrastructure**

Small business credit is growing at 26.18 percent this year and now represents 23.06 percent of all business lending. Growth in micro and small business lending jumped by 23.77 percentage points, powered by government systems like UDYAM registration and GST data that make it much easier to verify that tiny businesses are real and can repay loans. Banks cannot reach thousands of small borrowers on their own, so they need partners who can do the ground work of finding customers and checking their creditworthiness.

> So what: Banks should build partnerships with fintech companies and NBFCs who specialize in reaching small businesses, using these partners to handle customer acquisition and initial credit checks while the bank provides the funding.


**Gold loan market entry / deepening for banks**

Banks are growing gold loans by 105.5 percent compared to last year, and this growth has kept climbing for 12.0 periods in a row. Gold prices jumped sharply between 2023 and 2026, so customers can now borrow much more money using the same gold jewelry or coins as security. Banks are winning customers from finance companies because they have local branches where staff can check the gold is real and store it safely, plus they charge lower interest rates.

> So what: A bank should open more branches in neighborhoods where people own gold jewelry, train staff to test gold quickly and accurately, and offer simple gold loan products with fast approval to capture customers switching from finance companies.


**MSME origination infrastructure investment window**

Small business lending is growing at 32.68 pct per year, and these tiny businesses now make up 23.06 pct of all MSME lending. Their growth rate jumped by 23.77 pp recently, while bigger MSME borrowers are growing much slower—the gap is 11.8 pp. Banks can now check if these small businesses can repay loans using government registration systems and GST records, which makes lending safer and faster.

> So what: A bank should invest in systems that connect to UDYAM registration and GST databases so it can quickly approve loans for small businesses that were previously too risky to lend to.


## Live openings — Payments


**Credit card penetration deepening — PSB share gap is the opening**

India has 120454115.0 credit cards outstanding, growing at 8.47 percent year-on-year. Private banks hold 70.91 percent of all credit cards, while public sector banks hold only 23.83 percent — even though public banks control about 64 percent of debit cards. This means public banks already have millions of customers with debit cards who could be offered credit cards at much lower cost than finding completely new customers.

> So what: Public sector banks should launch simple credit card offers to their existing debit card customers, because they already know these people and the cost to convert them is far lower than chasing strangers.


**Rural cash distribution via Micro ATM / Business Correspondent networks**

India has 1358241.0 micro ATMs that let villagers withdraw cash without visiting a full branch. The number of these machines dropped -8.11 percent recently, meaning fewer are being added or some are closing. Even though the count is falling, people in small towns still need cash daily and a micro ATM costs far less to operate than opening a real branch.

> So what: A bank should keep investing in micro ATMs in villages where branches are too expensive, because villagers still depend on cash and these machines are much cheaper to run than full branches.


**Embedded transit and tap-to-pay payment products**

Credit card spending in the 'other' category jumped 2365.96 percent in value and 714.01 percent in volume year-on-year. People are tapping cards at transit gates, vending machines, and small shops that just installed tap-to-pay terminals. This is not existing cardholders spending more — millions of people are using cards in completely new places for the first time.

> So what: Banks should issue cards designed for these new tap-to-pay surfaces, partner with transit systems and small merchants to embed payment acceptance, and build simple card products for first-time users who want convenience at everyday touchpoints.


## Watch-outs


**Infrastructure repayment wave with insufficient new origination pipeline**

Banks over-indexed to traditional infrastructure credit face a structural squeeze: the FY22-24 project book moves to repayment (compressing balances) while the next-cycle pipeline (renewables, data centres) is slower to ramp. Banks without next-cycle project finance capability face credit portfolio contraction.

> So what: Move on this while the trend is still running in your favour.


**CIC reporting compliance gaps creating off-balance-sheet credit risk**

RBI Digital Lending Guidelines (2022/2025) require BNPL and digital credit to report to Credit Information Companies. However, compliance may be incomplete among newer digital lenders operating under first-loss guarantee structures or co-lending arrangements where the regulated entity's on-book exposure is partially shielded. If reporting gaps exist, the true unsecured credit stress is understated in bank balance sheets and in SIBC data.

> So what: Move on this while the trend is still running in your favour.


**Credit card market concentrated in top-5 private banks**

The top-5 private banks control approximately 71% of credit card outstanding. The largest single issuer holds ~20% of the market. This concentration creates systemic fragility — as demonstrated when a single payment bank's exit caused a market-wide debit card step-down. Similar disruption from a major credit card issuer would have proportionally larger effects.

> So what: Move on this while the trend is still running in your favour.
