# Credit Insights — RBI SIBC Mar 2026 (annual snapshot, published Apr 30 2026)

The April 2026 release is the first complete view of FY26. Three FY-end snapshots
(Mar 2024, Mar 2025, Mar 2026) make this the cleanest year-on-year comparison in
the SIBC series in months. Below: the seven things that matter for credit
professionals, with exact numbers.

---

## 1. The credit cycle is in its strongest expansion since the post-pandemic recovery

Bank credit reached **₹213.6L Cr** at Mar 2026. FY26 added **₹29.6L Cr** in
incremental credit; FY25 added **₹18.2L Cr**. Year-on-year growth went from
**11.0%** (FY25) to **16.1%** (FY26).

Every main sector accelerated: Agriculture 10.4 → 15.7%, Industry 8.2 → 15.0%,
Services 12.0 → 19.0%, Personal Loans 11.7 → 16.2%. There is no laggard.

**For lenders:** if your FY26 portfolio grew below 16%, you lost share in a
system-wide tailwind. Capital, not demand, is the binding constraint going into
FY27.

---

## 2. Services has overtaken Personal Loans as the fastest-growing main sector

Services grew **19.0% YoY** in FY26 — the highest of the four. Personal Loans
came in second at 16.2%. The single biggest driver: bank credit to NBFCs went
from **7.4% YoY** in FY25 (post-RBI risk-weight squeeze) to **26.3% YoY** in
FY26. Absolute incremental flow to NBFCs: **₹4.30L Cr** in FY26 versus ₹1.12L Cr
in FY25 — nearly 4x.

The 2023 risk-weight rise has been fully absorbed. NBFCs are once again the
dominant on-lending counterparty for retail and MSME credit.

**For lenders / fintechs:** co-origination, warehouse financing, and
loan-management infrastructure for bank-NBFC partnerships are the operative
product wedges for FY27.

---

## 3. Gold Loans grew 5x in 24 months — but most of it is reclassification, not new demand

Gold Loans went from **₹0.93L Cr** (Mar 2024) to **₹2.06L Cr** (Mar 2025) to
**₹4.60L Cr** (Mar 2026). Two consecutive YoY rates of ~120% (121.1% then
123.1%).

Two compounding effects: RBI's Sep 2024 circular tightened gold-loan
classification — bullet-repayment loans previously booked as 'agri' or
'business' had to be reclassified into gold loans. Gold prices also rose ~30%,
expanding collateral value on existing books.

Gold loan share of personal credit went from **1.7% to 6.6%** in two years.

**For lenders:** specialised gold-loan NBFCs (Manappuram, Muthoot, IIFL Finance)
and southern banks (Federal, KVB) own the disbursement velocity. Banks struggle
operationally with physical-collateral handling at this pace. Build product
partnerships, not direct competition.

---

## 4. The unsecured slowdown is real — credit cards are growing at 3.5%, the lowest in years

Credit Card Outstanding grew **3.5% YoY** in FY26, down from **10.6%** in FY25.
Incremental flow: **₹10,094 Cr** in FY26 versus ₹27,350 Cr in FY25 — under
one-third the pace.

Consumer Durables credit is in absolute contraction: -1.0% in FY25, **-5.3% in
FY26**. The book shrunk from ₹23,445 Cr to ₹21,962 Cr. RBI's 2023 risk-weight
increases on unsecured retail are biting.

**For issuers:** revenue mix needs to pivot toward fee, interchange and float —
not net-interest income on revolving balances. CD-financing as a standalone
product category is shrinking; volume has migrated to embedded BNPL and card
EMI.

---

## 5. MSME credit is the highest-velocity wallet in the system

Industry-by-size view: **Micro and Small grew 33.1% YoY** in FY26 — up from
8.9% in FY25. Medium grew 21.7%. Large grew only 8.9%. The PSL view confirms:
MSE credit grew **29.5% YoY** in FY26 (₹22.4L Cr → ₹29.0L Cr).

This is partly real demand and partly formalisation. GST and UDYAM enrolment
have brought millions of informal enterprises into the formal financial system
over the past 4–5 years. Many of these new borrowers have thin or blank bureau
histories.

**For lenders:** alternative-data underwriting (GST, UPI, e-way bills) is no
longer optional — it is the only way to underwrite the fastest-growing book.

---

## 6. Capex credit is broadening beyond Engineering

Industrial credit acceleration in FY26 by sub-type:

| Sub-type | FY25 YoY | FY26 YoY | Mar 2026 size |
|---|---|---|---|
| All Engineering | 22.0% | 32.2% | ₹3.17L Cr |
| Petroleum, Coal | 16.5% | 32.5% | ₹2.04L Cr |
| Basic Metal | 12.8% | 19.4% | ₹5.18L Cr |
| Cement | -0.0% | 9.0% | ₹0.78L Cr |
| Chemicals | 7.4% | 14.9% | ₹3.08L Cr |
| Vehicles & Parts | 5.2% | 18.1% | ₹1.41L Cr |

The PLI capex story has spread well beyond electronics. Petroleum, Basic Metal,
and Cement turning up signals a broad-based industrial capex revival.

**Infrastructure** remains the laggard at **9.5% YoY** — still the largest
industrial book at ₹14.9L Cr but barely expanding. The FY21-24 highway/metro
supercycle has matured; data centres and green hydrogen have not yet reached
scale.

---

## 7. The PSL Housing 39.8% surge is a definition change, not real housing demand

Priority Sector Housing went from **-1.1% YoY** in FY25 to **+39.8% YoY** in
FY26. The book jumped from ₹7.47L Cr to ₹10.44L Cr.

This is almost certainly the result of RBI's October 2024 PSL housing limit
revision (metro: ₹35L → ₹45L; non-metro: ₹25L → ₹35L). Existing housing loans
that fell outside the prior PSL bracket got reclassified IN — they are not new
originations.

The 'real' housing trajectory is in the personalLoans.Housing line at **11.5%
YoY** (₹30.1L Cr → ₹33.6L Cr), which is steady mid-teens compounding.

**For analysts:** strip the PSL Housing line from any quote about housing
demand. Use personalLoans.Housing as the underlying lending signal.

# Credit Data Gaps — RBI SIBC Mar 2026 (annual snapshot, published Apr 30 2026)

This file is unusual in the SIBC series. It contains only three FY-end
snapshots (Mar 2024, Mar 2025, Mar 2026) — not the typical monthly cadence —
and uses split date conventions across statements. Below: the seven gaps a
reader needs to be aware of before quoting any number.

---

## 1. Bank Credit dates are early-April fortnights, not Mar-end

**What the data shows on the surface:** Bank Credit, Food Credit, and Non-food
Credit appear with three time points labelled "Apr 2024", "Apr 2025", "Mar
2026".

**Why it's misleading:** RBI publishes Bank Credit on a fortnightly cycle. The
"Apr 2024" date is **Apr 5, 2024** — the FY24-end fortnight; "Apr 2025" is **Apr
4, 2025** — the FY25-end fortnight. Sub-sectors (Agriculture, Industry,
Services, Personal Loans) use Mar 22, 2024 and Mar 21, 2025 — different actual
dates inside the same statement.

**What a reader should do:** Treat all three Bank Credit dates as FY-end
snapshots. The labelling differs but the values are like-for-like across years.
The 16.1% growth quoted is the file's published variation column (Mar 31, 2026
versus Apr 4, 2025) — not a recomputed YoY.

---

## 2. FY-to-date growth is empty for the bank credit aggregate

**What the data shows on the surface:** The dashboard's FY view is blank for
bankCredit (only Bank Credit, Food Credit, Non-food Credit lines).

**Why it's misleading:** FY-to-date growth requires a March base column to
anchor against. Because Bank Credit's earlier columns are early-April
fortnights, there is no internal March anchor for the aggregate. FY data
displays correctly for sectors 1-4 but not for the bank credit aggregate.

**What a reader should do:** Use the Absolute or YoY views for the bank credit
aggregate — not FY. The 16.1% headline growth uses RBI's published variation
column.

---

## 3. Three data points limit within-FY pacing analysis

**What the data shows on the surface:** A clean 3-point progression Mar 2024 →
Mar 2025 → Mar 2026.

**Why it's misleading:** Year-end-to-year-end growth can hide whether the
acceleration was front-loaded (Apr-Sep 2025) or back-loaded (Jan-Mar 2026).
Lenders pricing FY27 risk need to know which.

**What a reader should do:** Supplement this file with the consolidated CSV
that has Jan/Feb monthly snapshots for FY26. Use this file for clean YoY
comparisons; use the monthly stream for trajectory.

---

## 4. The four main sectors don't sum to total bank credit

**What the data shows on the surface:** Agriculture + Industry + Services +
Personal Loans for Mar 2026 sums to ₹202.3L Cr.

**Why it's misleading:** Total Bank Credit is ₹213.6L Cr at Mar 2026. Adding
Food Credit (₹0.70L Cr) brings the visible total to ₹203.0L Cr — leaving
**₹10.6L Cr of "other" non-food lending** invisible in the main-sector view.
This is roughly 5% of bank credit — small business loans, public-sector
advances, and other categories not classified into the four selected sectors.

**What a reader should do:** Treat the main-sector view as "selected sectors"
coverage. For full system accounting, anchor on Bank Credit total; for thematic
analysis, use the sector breakdown.

---

## 5. PSL Housing's 39.8% surge is a classification event, not new demand

**What the data shows on the surface:** Priority Sector Housing grew from
-1.1% YoY in FY25 to +39.8% YoY in FY26. The book jumped ₹7.47L Cr →
₹10.44L Cr.

**Why it's misleading:** RBI revised PSL housing limits in October 2024
(metro: ₹35L → ₹45L; non-metro: ₹25L → ₹35L). Existing housing loans that
previously fell outside the PSL bracket got reclassified IN. They are not new
originations — they are loans that already existed, now showing in a different
column.

**What a reader should do:** Never quote PSL Housing as a demand signal. Use
personalLoans.Housing (+11.5% YoY) as the real housing trajectory. PSL line
shifts of 30+ percentage points in a single year are almost always definition
changes.

---

## 6. Most of Gold Loans' 123% growth is reclassification + price, not new lending

**What the data shows on the surface:** Gold Loans went 5x from ₹0.93L Cr to
₹4.60L Cr in 24 months.

**Why it's misleading:** RBI's Sep 2024 circular tightened gold-loan
classification — bullet-repayment loans previously booked under "agri" or
"business" categories had to be reclassified into "loans against gold
jewellery". Gold prices also rose ~30% over the period, expanding the
collateral value of existing loan books.

Both effects compound: more loans visible AS gold loans, each loan secured by
collateral worth more. The 123% YoY rate overstates real new disbursement
demand.

**What a reader should do:** When forecasting gold-loan trajectory into FY27,
separate stock effect (reclassification, fully recognised by Mar 2026) from
flow effect (real new disbursements). Stress-test for a gold-price reversal
scenario; collateral-value contraction would shrink reported balances.

---

## 7. Bank credit to NBFCs is double-counted in any system aggregate

**What the data shows on the surface:** NBFC credit (within Services) is
₹20.66L Cr at Mar 2026 — the largest single line in the Services category.

**Why it's misleading:** Bank credit to NBFCs becomes NBFC on-lending to retail
and MSME borrowers. Those downstream loans appear again in personal-loan or
industrial-loan tables published by NBFCs themselves. There is no system-wide
deduplicated view of end-borrower credit.

**What a reader should do:** When triangulating debt-to-income or debt-to-GDP
ratios, deduct bank-to-NBFC flow first. Headline household-leverage numbers
that add bank credit + NBFC credit are systematically overstated.

# Lending Opportunities — RBI SIBC Mar 2026 (annual snapshot, published Apr 30 2026)

Five highest-conviction opportunities visible in the FY26 data. Each is
grounded in absolute flow numbers, not generic theme-spotting. Conviction is
ranked by combined velocity and size of the underlying wallet.

---

## 1. Co-lending and warehouse-financing infrastructure for the bank-NBFC cycle

**The opportunity:** With bank credit to NBFCs growing **26.3% YoY** and
incremental flow of **₹4.30L Cr in FY26 alone**, the bilateral co-lending and
structured warehouse arrangements between banks and NBFCs are the most active
product wedge in the system. Banks need NBFC origination reach; NBFCs need
balance sheet capacity.

**Data evidence:** NBFCs (Services line) at ₹20.66L Cr (Mar 2026), up from
₹15.23L Cr (Mar 2024). FY25 added ₹1.12L Cr; FY26 added ₹4.30L Cr — nearly 4x
the prior-year flow.

**Best-positioned lender:** Private banks with strong NBFC relationships (Axis,
ICICI, HDFC, IndusInd, Federal). For tech and product builders: loan-management
systems with co-lending support, partition logic, and audit-trail rigour have a
12-18 month buying window before in-house tooling at large banks fills the gap.

**Key risk / caveat:** RBI could re-tighten risk weights again if it perceives
NBFC sector overheating. Watch H1 FY27 RBI Financial Stability Reports closely.

---

## 2. Alternative-data underwriting stack for thin-file MSMEs

**The opportunity:** Micro and Small industrial credit grew **33.1% YoY** in
FY26 — the largest single acceleration in the file. Absolute add was
**₹2.65L Cr in FY26 alone**, four times the FY25 flow. A meaningful share of
this is first-cycle formal borrowers (post-GST, post-UDYAM) with thin or blank
bureau histories. Bureau-only underwriting cannot scale to this book.

**Data evidence:** Industry-by-size view: Micro and Small at ₹10.63L Cr (Mar
2026), up from ₹7.98L Cr (Mar 2025). PSL MSE confirms the trajectory:
₹29.0L Cr at Mar 2026, +29.5% YoY.

**Best-positioned lender:** MSME-focused NBFCs (Bajaj Finance, Cholamandalam,
Aditya Birla Capital), small-finance banks (Equitas, Suryoday, Bandhan), and
B2B fintechs (Indifi, Lendingkart, Capital Float). For tech builders: data
partnerships with GSPs and account aggregators are the leverage point — alt-data
underwriting models trained on GST, UPI, and e-way-bill signals.

**Key risk / caveat:** First-cycle MSME borrowers have no through-the-cycle
performance history. FY27-28 will be the first stress test for alternative-data
underwriting models. Loss curves could surprise.

---

## 3. Specialised gold-loan operations at scale

**The opportunity:** Gold Loans grew 5x in two years to ₹4.60L Cr — the most
dramatic personal-credit reclassification in recent memory. Banks struggle
operationally with the physical-collateral handling, valuation, and short-tenor
cadence that gold loans require. Specialised NBFCs and tier-3/4 banks own the
disbursement velocity.

**Data evidence:** Gold Loans at ₹4.60L Cr (Mar 2026), up from ₹2.06L Cr (Mar
2025) and ₹0.93L Cr (Mar 2024). Two consecutive YoY rates above 120%. Gold
share of personal credit: 1.7% → 6.6% in 24 months.

**Best-positioned lender:** Pure-play gold NBFCs (Manappuram, Muthoot, IIFL
Finance) and southern banks with branch density (Federal Bank, KVB, South
Indian Bank, Karnataka Bank). For new entrants: partnership models with gold
NBFCs are operationally faster than building branch operations from zero.

**Key risk / caveat:** A material gold-price reversal (>20% decline) would
trigger collateral re-valuation across the book. LTV ratios on the marginal
loan are already aggressive; stock effect from past reclassifications would
amplify reported book contraction in a downturn scenario.

---

## 4. Vehicle-supply-chain financing for the EV and Tier-2 component build-out

**The opportunity:** Two parallel signals point at the same theme. Vehicle
Loans (personal) accelerated from 8.6% to **18.6% YoY** in FY26 — over 2x the
absolute pace. Vehicles, Vehicle Parts and Transport Equipment industrial
credit jumped from 5.2% to **18.1% YoY**. The OEM EV ramp-up (Tata, Mahindra,
Hyundai-India) is now propagating into Tier-1 and Tier-2 component capex.

**Data evidence:** Vehicle Loans (personal): ₹7.39L Cr at Mar 2026, +18.6%.
Vehicles & Parts (industrial): ₹1.41L Cr at Mar 2026, +18.1% YoY. FY26 incremental
on personal vehicle loans alone was ₹1.16L Cr.

**Best-positioned lender:** OEM captive NBFCs (Bajaj Finance, M&M Finance, Tata
Motors Finance) on the personal side. On the supply-chain side: TReDS platforms
(M1xchange, Invoicemart, RXIL), bilateral supply-chain finance teams at private
banks, and specialist NBFCs (Mahindra Finance Vendor, Cholamandalam SCF).

**Key risk / caveat:** EV adoption rates lagging policy targets could compress
Tier-1 component capex pipelines. Watch Q2 FY27 PV and 2W EV penetration data
closely.

---

## 5. Capex term-loan books for industrial revival across Petroleum, Engineering, and Metals

**The opportunity:** The PLI capex story has spread well beyond electronics in
FY26. All Engineering grew 32.2%, Petroleum 32.5%, Basic Metal 19.4%, Cement
turned from -0.0% to +9.0%, Vehicles & Parts to 18.1%. PSU banks
underweight industrial term loans have under-priced upside; private banks
likely already over-allocated.

**Data evidence (FY26 YoY):**
- All Engineering: 22.0 → 32.2% (₹3.17L Cr)
- Petroleum, Coal: 16.5 → 32.5% (₹2.04L Cr)
- Basic Metal: 12.8 → 19.4% (₹5.18L Cr)
- Cement: -0.0 → 9.0% (₹0.78L Cr)
- Chemicals: 7.4 → 14.9% (₹3.08L Cr)
- Food Processing: 5.1 → 14.0% (₹2.50L Cr)

**Best-positioned lender:** PSU banks with project-finance teams (SBI, Bank of
Baroda, Canara) for large-ticket term loans. Private banks (Axis, ICICI, Kotak)
for working-capital tie-ins to capex projects. Specialised infrastructure
financiers (REC, PFC, IIFCL) for clean-tech and renewable-adjacent capex.

**Key risk / caveat:** Industrial credit has historically been the most
cyclical book. A consumption slowdown in H2 FY27 would hit working capital
draws first. Staircase-based EAD modelling and sensitivity analysis around
demand assumptions are essential.

# Data Gaps — RBI SIBC Merged (Jan 2024 – Mar 2026)

> Period: Jan 2024 – Mar 2026 | Updated: 2026-05-02
> Source: RBI SIBC merged across all ingested periods (Jan 2026, Feb 2026, Mar 2026 files)

---

## 1. January SIBC files do not report Gold Loans or PSL Housing sub-classification

Gold Loans and PSL Housing both appear as null in all January data rows. These series only exist in the March SIBC files. As a result, Jan→Jan YoY comparisons for gold loans and PSL housing are not possible — the cross-period acceleration story for these categories can only be told using March data points.

**Impact:** Any analysis of gold loan trends using merged data must explicitly note that January figures are absent. Readers cannot assess whether the March-to-March trajectory holds in interim months.

---

## 2. January SIBC files do not report NBFC credit or Tourism sub-classification in Services

NBFC credit and Tourism are null in all January service rows. Only March SIBC files capture NBFC sub-classification. Since NBFCs are the largest single services sub-sector, January services totals do not reflect the full picture. The merged Jan→Jan services YoY comparison excludes the NBFC channel.

**Impact:** Services growth rates in January-to-January comparisons understate the true services credit trajectory if NBFC credit grew faster than the other sub-sectors in the year.

---

## 3. Food credit seasonal artifact distorts January bank credit totals

Food credit (NABARD-channelled agricultural procurement credit) peaks in January at kharif marketing season and drops sharply by March. This makes January bank credit totals appear larger than the March figures for the same fiscal year, distorting FY-to-date growth calculations that use January as the base.

**Correct signal:** Non-food credit is the right metric for January-to-January comparisons. Food credit should be stripped from all trend analyses using January data.

---

## 4. PSL Housing +39.8% YoY (FY26) is reclassification, not organic lending

PSL Housing: ₹7.47L Cr (Mar 2025) → ₹10.44L Cr (Mar 2026), +39.8% YoY. The Oct 2024 RBI revision raised PSL housing loan ceilings (metro: ₹35L → ₹45L, non-metro: ₹25L → ₹35L). Loans already on bank books that previously exceeded the old limits now qualify as PSL. The ₹2.97L Cr appearing as credit growth is reclassification of existing exposures, not new lending. Confirmed at FY-end.

**Impact:** PSL Housing growth rates for FY26 are not comparable to prior years. Never cite PSL Housing as a housing demand signal. Use personalLoans.Housing (+11.5% YoY) instead.

## 5. PSL totals are not additive across sub-categories

PSL sub-category totals (Housing + Micro & Small + Export Credit + Education + Renewable Energy + Others) do not sum to the PSL aggregate. The discrepancy arises because some loans qualify under multiple PSL categories and are counted in each. Treating sub-category figures as exhaustive slices produces incorrect totals.

**Impact:** Cross-category PSL market share analysis must use category-specific figures, not derived shares from the aggregate.

---

## 6. Consumer Durables YoY deepening contraction is not visible in merged January data alone

Consumer Durables: -1.0% YoY (Jan 2025) → -9.6% YoY (Jan 2026). The worsening trend is only visible with the Jan→Jan comparison enabled by merged data. Any single-period analysis would see a contraction but could not determine whether it was accelerating or stabilizing.

**Impact:** The merged view is essential for distinguishing trend from noise for small, volatile categories like consumer durables. Without it, the policy causation (RBI risk-weight hike driving accelerating contraction) cannot be confirmed.

---

## 7. Other Services and Other Personal Loans remain opaque across all periods

"Other Services" and "Other Personal Loans" are residual catch-all categories. Neither SIBC file provides sub-classification. These residuals are large enough to materially affect sector totals but their composition is unknown across all merged periods.

**Impact:** Any significant change in these residuals cannot be attributed to a structural driver without supplementary data (BSR-1, credit bureau data). They are black boxes in the causal model.

---

## 8. Gems & Jewellery credit conflates gold price appreciation with volume growth

Gems & Jewellery: ₹0.73L Cr (Jan 2025) → ₹0.91L Cr (Jan 2026), +24.7% YoY. Gold prices rose approximately 40% in the same period. If working capital loans are collateral-based and LTV-constrained, a significant portion of the growth is gold price appreciation inflating collateral values and loan amounts — not volume expansion.

**Impact:** Gems & Jewellery YoY growth overstates genuine business expansion during gold price surges. The real growth rate (volume-adjusted) is likely lower.

---

## 9. Bank credit aggregate uses April fortnight dates — not March-end

Bank Credit aggregate columns are labelled 'Apr 2024' (Apr 5, 2024) and 'Apr 2025' (Apr 4, 2025) because RBI publishes fortnightly. Sub-sectors use Mar 22/21. The Mar 2026 annual file uses Mar 31, 2026. The published 16.1% YoY variation uses RBI's own variation column.

**Impact:** Do not mix Bank Credit totals with sector sub-totals in arithmetic sums — the dates are not identical. The Apr→Apr comparison used for the Bank Credit aggregate is subtly different from the Mar→Mar used for sectors.

---

## 10. Gold loans 5× overstates new disbursement demand — two one-time stock effects

Two compounding effects inflated the 123% YoY: (1) RBI Sep 2024 circular required bullet-repayment gold loans to migrate from agri/business categories; (2) gold prices rose ~30%, expanding collateral value on existing books. Both are one-time stock effects, fully recognised by Mar 2026.

**Impact:** When forecasting gold-loan trajectory into FY27, separate stock effect (reclassification, done) from flow effect (real new disbursements). Stress-test for gold price reversal >20% — LTV ratios on marginal loans are already aggressive.

## 2. Alternative-data MSME underwriting — FY-end confirmation makes this non-negotiable

**Signal strength: Very High (33.1% YoY confirmed at FY-end, two independent series aligned)**

Micro & Small YoY: +9.6% (FY25) → **+33.1% (FY26, Mar 2026)**. PSL MSE: +13.4% (FY25) → **+29.5% (FY26, Mar 2026)**. Two independent series, confirmed at year-end. FY26 incremental: ₹2.65L Cr — 4× the FY25 add.

**The opportunity:** A meaningful share of this growth is first-cycle formal borrowers post-GST and UDYAM with thin or blank bureau histories. Bureau-only underwriting cannot scale to this book. Alt-data models (GST returns, UPI transaction history, e-way-bill volumes) are the only way to underwrite the fastest-growing portfolio in the system.

**The window:** GST-registered MSMEs from 2019-22 now have 4-6 years of digital financial history — enough for model training. FY27-28 will be the first stress test for alt-data models; first-cycle borrower loss curves could surprise.

**Action:** Partner with GSPs (GST Suvidha Providers) and account aggregators for data access. Train underwriting models on 4+ years of GST and UPI data. Target Medium enterprises (+21.7% YoY) as a bridge — they have richer data and lower first-cycle risk than Micro.

**Action:** Identify top-5 PLI anchor companies in electronics (Apple ecosystem) and defence (HAL, DRDO supply chain). Offer supply chain finance with anchor payment confirmation as credit enhancement. Structure as off-balance-sheet where possible for capital efficiency.
