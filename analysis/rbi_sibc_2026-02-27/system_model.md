# System Model — RBI SIBC Jan 2026

> Why the credit system looks the way it does. Causal chains, structural shifts, pressure points, and where this is heading.

---

## 1. The Current State

Total bank credit stands at ₹204.8L Cr as of Jan 2026, growing at +14.6% YoY — the fastest rate in this dataset. Non-food credit added ₹25.8L Cr in the last 12 months alone, up from ₹18.1L Cr added in the prior year. The system is in an accelerating growth phase.

The composition has shifted decisively toward retail: personal loans (₹67.2L Cr, 33%) and services (₹57.2L Cr, 28%) now account for 61% of sectoral credit and are both growing above the system average. Industry (₹43.9L Cr, 21%) and agriculture (₹25.1L Cr, 12%) are growing but losing share. Within industry, the growth is entirely in small and medium businesses — large corporate credit is near-stagnant at +5.5% YoY.

The most anomalous signal in the system is gold loans: +128.8% YoY, growing 4.4x in 24 months. This is simultaneously a lending boom and a household stress indicator.

---

## 2. What's Driving It

**A. The formalisation dividend is arriving in credit data**

GST registration and UDYAM enrolment since 2018–2022 created a large cohort of businesses that existed informally and are now visible to the formal credit system. Their transactions are GST-verified. Their income is TDS-documented. They have bank accounts with 2–3 years of history. They have never had a formal loan.

This cohort is now actively converting to bank credit. It explains why Micro & Small grew +31.2% YoY while the broader economy didn't grow anywhere near that rate. The formalisation dividend is being harvested right now. It will taper in 3–5 years as this cohort exhausts its first-time borrowing opportunity and the remaining unregistered businesses diminish.

**B. Regulatory pressure on unsecured credit has rerouted growth**

RBI's November 2023 risk-weight increase on unsecured consumer credit (100% → 125%) put a direct cost on credit card and personal loan growth. The effect is visible in the data: credit cards went from +13% YoY to +1.5% in 12 months. Consumer durables lending is declining (-4.0% YoY). The credit demand didn't disappear — it migrated to products RBI is friendlier toward: gold loans (secured), vehicle loans (secured), housing (long-tenor, priority-sector eligible).

This is not a temporary shift. The regulatory environment signals that secured lending will continue to have lower capital costs than unsecured. The credit mix is adjusting accordingly and will continue to adjust.

**C. Household financial stress is monetising gold holdings**

India holds an estimated 25,000 tonnes of gold in household savings — the largest stock of privately-held gold in the world. Most of it has never been pledged. The 4.4x surge in gold loans in 24 months is partly a structural shift (formalisation of gold lending, digital origination making it easier) and partly a demand signal (households needing liquidity, especially post-COVID income disruption and elevated inflation in 2022–24).

The two drivers have different implications. The structural driver (easier access, digital origination) is permanent and benign. The stress driver (households pledging out of necessity) creates a portfolio risk if economic conditions tighten and borrowers cannot service or redeem their gold.

**D. Industrial policy is creating a manufacturing credit demand cycle**

India's PLI scheme, capex incentives, and supply chain diversification from China are generating real new investment in manufacturing. All Engineering grew +35.9% YoY, Chemicals +15.1%, Basic Metal +13.8%. This is not financial engineering — it's companies borrowing to build factories, buy machinery, and fund supply chains. The capex cycle has a multi-year runway as PLI commitments are fulfilled and supply chain localisation deepens.

**E. The NBFC layer is expanding the credit system's reach**

Banks cannot cost-effectively serve microfinance customers, small informal businesses, or rural borrowers directly. NBFCs — funded by banks at ₹19.05L Cr and growing +17.8% YoY — are the intermediary layer that extends credit into segments the banking system can't reach on its own. This wholesale funding-to-retail-lending chain is how India's credit system scales beyond what bank branch economics allow.

---

## 3. Structural Shifts — What Has Changed Irreversibly

**Retail > Corporate as the primary market.** Personal loans surpassed industry credit in size and the gap is widening (₹23.3L Cr gap in Jan 2026 vs ₹16.2L Cr in Jan 2024). This will not reverse. Large corporate borrowers have increasingly accessed capital markets, ECBs, and internal accruals rather than bank credit. The bank credit market is a retail and MSME market now.

**Gold as mainstream collateral.** Gold loans were a niche product of South Indian NBFCs a decade ago. At ₹4.01L Cr growing +128.8%, it is now a mainstream retail credit category. Banks have entered (SBI, Canara, HDFC Bank). The normalisation of pledging gold for credit — once stigmatised in many communities — appears to be a permanent behavioural change.

**Small business credit > large corporate credit.** Micro & Small grew 43.8% over 2 years vs Large's 12.6%. This is not cyclical. Large companies are choosing capital markets over bank credit for their marginal financing needs. MSME is where bank credit is growing, and the formalisation pipeline will sustain this for several more years.

**BNPL/fintech displacement of small-ticket consumer credit.** Consumer durables lending at banks is declining every quarter (-4.0% YoY). The EMI financing of electronics, appliances, and small purchases has moved to fintech platforms. Banks have essentially exited this segment without formally deciding to — they just became uncompetitive and stopped growing while fintechs took the volume.

---

## 4. Pressure Points — Where the System Is Stressed or About to Shift

**Gold loan quality through the cycle.** The surge from ₹0.92L Cr to ₹4.01L Cr in 24 months means a large volume of gold loans were originated at gold prices near their all-time highs. If gold prices fall 20–30% (not unprecedented), LTV ratios across the portfolio will breach regulatory limits simultaneously. The industry would face forced auctions at scale — exactly the situation RBI flagged in 2024 when it found LTV violations at multiple lenders. Gold loan quality is the single largest latent risk in retail banking right now.

**NBFC concentration risk.** Banks have ₹19.05L Cr of credit exposure to NBFCs growing +17.8% annually. The top 20 NBFCs likely represent 80%+ of this. If any large NBFC faces liquidity stress (as several did in 2018–19 after IL&FS), the contagion effect on bank credit is immediate. The system is more NBFC-dependent than it was 5 years ago, and this dependency is growing.

**Unsecured personal loan books at fintechs and small banks.** Although bank-level credit card data shows stagnation, the fintech-originated unsecured personal loan market (not fully visible in this RBI dataset) has been growing rapidly. RBI's risk-weight action was aimed partly at this. If credit quality in these books deteriorates — particularly in ₹10,000–₹50,000 short-tenor loans — it could trigger another regulatory tightening, affecting fintechs and the NBFCs that fund them.

**PSL compliance pressure.** Banks are required to direct 40% of net bank credit to priority sectors. With total bank credit growing at 14.6% YoY, the absolute PSL target is rising faster than banks can organically originate qualifying loans in some categories. The result is growing demand for PSLCs (Priority Sector Lending Certificates) — effectively banks paying a premium to buy PSL credit from lenders who can originate in those segments. Lenders with genuine PSL origination capability (MFIs, HFCs in affordable housing, agricultural NBFCs) have a structural advantage: they generate PSL inventory they can sell at a premium.

## 5. The 2-Year View — Where This Is Likely Heading

**Credit growth stays above 12%, probably reaches 15–16% in FY26.** The formalisation-driven MSME demand, PLI capex cycle, and gold loan volume are all multi-year tailwinds. Unless RBI tightens rates or imposes further sector-specific restrictions, the system's growth momentum will continue.

**Gold loans double again.** At +128.8% annual growth, ₹4.01L Cr becomes ₹9L Cr by Jan 2027 — at current rates. Even at a much slower 40–50% growth (natural deceleration as the market matures), gold loans cross ₹6–7L Cr. This segment becomes comparable in size to vehicle loans and a top-3 personal loan sub-category. RBI will likely introduce more detailed LTV and underwriting standards as the book scales.

**MSME growth moderates but stays above system average.** The formalisation dividend tapers as the first-time-borrower pool shrinks. YoY growth for Micro & Small likely decelerates from +31.2% back toward 15–20% as the base effect normalises. Medium enterprises continue growing at 18–22% as they represent a genuinely underpenetrated segment with deepening formal banking relationships.

**A credit event in one NBFC forces restructuring of the bank-NBFC funding model.** This is a when-not-if prediction. The ₹19.05L Cr exposure growing at 17.8% will produce concentration risk that leads to a credit event at one large NBFC within 3–5 years. RBI will likely respond by requiring banks to diversify NBFC counterparty exposure more formally, or by directly regulating large NBFC funding structures. This will temporarily slow NBFC credit growth and push banks to build more direct retail capability.

**Infrastructure credit stays slow unless power projects accelerate.** Roads, telecom, and railways are structurally slow in this credit cycle. Power is the only infrastructure sub-sector with active project pipelines. Unless a new infrastructure spending programme launches (Budget 2026–27 announcements could change this), infrastructure will continue to drag on industry credit growth and cede share to MSME.

**The affordable housing opportunity will be captured by whoever builds Tier 3/4 distribution first.** PSL housing at +37.9% YoY signals real policy momentum. The demand exists. The PMAY pipeline is real. The lender who builds or buys distribution infrastructure in 200 semi-urban towns in FY26–27 will be very difficult to displace — origination relationships in smaller towns are sticky.
