# Credit Insights — RBI SIBC Jan 2026

> The 7 most important things the data shows. Each with exact numbers, why it matters now, and one implication for a lender or fintech.

---

## 1. Gold Loans: 4.4x in 24 Months — The Single Biggest Signal in This Data

**The observation:** Loans against gold jewellery grew from ₹0.915L Cr (Jan 2024) to ₹1.75L Cr (Jan 2025) to ₹4.01L Cr (Jan 2026). That's a 338% increase in 24 months. The YoY growth rate itself is accelerating: +91.4% (Jan 2025), +121.1% (Mar 2025), +128.8% (Jan 2026). Gold prices rose roughly 25–30% over the same period. Credit grew 4.4x while the collateral value moved 1.3x. That means far more people are pledging gold, not just the same people pledging more valuable gold.

**Why it matters now:** When large numbers of people pledge their household gold, it typically signals liquidity stress — gold is the lender of last resort for Indian households. This is simultaneously the fastest-growing credit product in retail banking and a stress signal for household financial health. The two facts are not in contradiction. The business opportunity is real. The risk profile of the average gold loan borrower entering the market in 2025–26 is materially different from the borrower who built this market in 2022–23.

**Implication for lenders:** Any lender without a gold loan product is watching a segment grow 10x faster than the market. But entering now requires thinking carefully about who the new borrower is. Income overlay on gold loan underwriting — using GST turnover, UPI flows, salary credits — separates stress-driven borrowers from asset-rich borrowers within the same LTV band. The lenders who price that distinction will do better through the cycle.

## 2. Personal Loans Overtook Corporate Credit — And the Gap Is Widening

**The observation:** Personal loans at ₹67.2L Cr (Jan 2026) is now the largest single sector in Indian banking, ahead of Services (₹57.2L Cr), Industry (₹43.9L Cr), and Agriculture (₹25.1L Cr). Personal loans exceeded industry credit in Jan 2024 too (₹52.3L Cr vs ₹36.1L Cr), but the gap has grown: personal loans now exceed industry credit by ₹23.3L Cr, up from ₹16.2L Cr two years ago. Within personal loans, the composition is shifting: gold loans grew 338% while housing credit — 49% of the category — grew just 11.1%.

**Why it matters now:** This is not a new trend, but it has now crossed a threshold where it's the defining structural fact of Indian banking. A bank that allocates more relationship management time to large corporate clients than to retail is allocating effort to a shrinking share of the market. The growth is in retail, and within retail, it's in the faster-moving, shorter-tenor products — gold, vehicle, education — not the slow-moving housing book.

**Implication for lenders:** If you're building a lending business in India in 2026, your baseline assumption should be that retail is the primary market and corporates are secondary. The reverse was true in 2010. The banks that will gain share in the next 5 years are those with efficient retail origination, not larger corporate relationship teams.

## 3. Credit Cards Have Hit a Policy Ceiling

**The observation:** Credit card outstanding grew +1.5% YoY in Jan 2026 — after growing 13% in Jan 2025, 25%+ in 2023, and 30%+ in 2022. The category is ₹2.964L Cr, essentially flat from ₹2.921L Cr twelve months earlier. In FY terms (Jan 2026 vs Mar 2025): +4.2%.

**Why it matters now:** This is not cyclical — it is a direct consequence of the RBI's November 2023 risk-weight increase on unsecured consumer credit from 100% to 125%. That one regulatory change made it more capital-expensive for banks to issue and grow credit card portfolios. Banks responded by slowing origination and tightening limits. The regulation is not being reversed; the ceiling will stay. The unsecured credit growth that used to flow through credit cards is not disappearing — it is being re-routed into secured products (vehicle loans: +17.1%, gold: +128.8%) and regulated fintech products.

**Implication for lenders:** Credit card is not a growth product anymore. Banks with heavy credit card books need to look at where those customers — high-income, digitally active, creditworthy — should be served next. That's likely vehicle loans, travel loans, EV financing, and secured personal lines. Fintechs building credit card alternatives (BNPL, credit lines) face the same regulatory pressure if they grow large enough to attract RBI's attention.

## 4. Micro & Small Credit Tripled Its Growth Rate — Something Structural Changed

**The observation:** Micro and Small enterprise credit grew +9.6% YoY in Jan 2025. By Jan 2026, the same metric is +31.2% — a 3x acceleration in 12 months. In absolute terms: ₹7.86L Cr → ₹10.31L Cr, adding ₹2.45L Cr in a single year vs ₹0.69L Cr the year before. Medium enterprises also accelerated: +18.4% (Jan 2025) → +22.3% (Jan 2026). Meanwhile, Large corporate credit slowed to +5.5%.

**Why it matters now:** The 3x acceleration in Micro & Small is too sharp to be purely organic credit demand growth. It coincides with multiple UDYAM-linked policies, PSL target pressures on banks, and possibly a definitional change in what counts as Micro & Small under the revised MSME classification. Some portion is businesses newly visible in bank data for the first time; some is genuine credit expansion. Either way, the direction is clear: small business credit is where the action is in industry lending.

**Implication for lenders:** The lending opportunity in industry has shifted decisively to the smaller end. Large corporate credit grew 12.6% over two years; Micro & Small grew 43.8%. The challenge is underwriting — many of these borrowers have thin or blank bureau files. The edge goes to lenders who can use alternative data (GST filings, UPI history, TReDS invoices) instead of requiring 3 years of ITR and physical collateral.

## 5. Services + Personal Loans = 61% of Credit, Both Growing Above Average

**The observation:** Services (₹57.2L Cr) and Personal Loans (₹67.2L Cr) together account for ₹124.5L Cr — 61% of total sectoral bank credit. Both are growing above the system average: Services at +15.5% YoY, Personal Loans at +14.9% YoY (system average: +14.6%). Industry and Agriculture, at 34% of credit combined, are both growing below average (+12.1% and +11.4% respectively).

**Why it matters now:** The credit system has two engines and two laggards. The engines — services and retail — are self-reinforcing: NBFCs (within Services) fund retail segments that banks can't reach, and rising personal credit supports consumption that feeds services revenue. The laggards — industry and agriculture — are not in crisis, but they're losing share year on year. Any lender whose book skews toward industry and agriculture is growing at 11–12% in a 14–15% market.

**Implication for lenders:** The portfolio mix question has a clear answer in this data. Services sub-sectors — NBFCs, Trade, Commercial Real Estate — and personal loan sub-categories — gold, vehicle, education — are where the credit system is adding volume. Teams, products, and credit policies should be calibrated for these segments first.

## 6. Engineering Credit Surged 35.9% — A Manufacturing Capex Cycle Has Started

**The observation:** All Engineering grew from ₹1.94L Cr (Jan 2024) to ₹2.56L Cr (Jan 2025) to ₹3.11L Cr (Jan 2026) — +60.5% in 24 months. The YoY acceleration is consistent: +18.0% (Jan 2025), +22.0% (Mar 2025), +35.9% (Jan 2026). This is the highest growth of any industry sub-sector with a balance above ₹1L Cr.

**Why it matters now:** India's Production Linked Incentive (PLI) scheme is creating genuine new manufacturing capacity across electronics, pharmaceuticals, auto components, and specialty chemicals. Companies that qualify for PLI subsidies are investing in new plants, machinery, and supply chains. That capex is being partly funded by bank credit. Three consecutive periods of accelerating growth suggests this is not a one-off project — it's a structural demand increase tied to industrial policy.

**Implication for lenders:** Engineering supply chains offer a well-structured lending opportunity. PLI anchor buyers have large, creditworthy balance sheets and verified supply chains. Their Tier 1 and Tier 2 suppliers need working capital to fulfil orders. These invoices can be verified through GST data and TReDS. The credit risk is lower than unsecured MSME lending at comparable yields because the anchor buyer provides a natural backstop.

## 7. PSL Housing: Went From Flat to +37.9% in One Data Point

**The observation:** Priority Sector housing credit was declining or flat for two consecutive periods: -1.3% YoY (Jan 2025), -1.1% YoY (Mar 2025). In Jan 2026 it shows +37.9% YoY, ₹10.31L Cr vs ₹7.47L Cr — an addition of ₹2.84L Cr in 12 months. This is the sharpest acceleration of any PSL sub-category in the dataset and is the opposite direction from where the data was pointing.

**Why it matters now:** A -1% to +38% reversal in 12 months in a ₹10L Cr category is unusual enough to warrant a closer look before accepting it at face value. Three possibilities: (1) PMAY scheme disbursals accelerated due to policy push; (2) banks reclassified existing housing loans as PSL-eligible after a definitional or threshold change; (3) genuine new origination in affordable housing picked up sharply. The implication differs significantly depending on which explanation is correct.

**Implication for lenders:** If the growth is real, the affordable housing opportunity in Tier 3 and 4 cities is very large and confirmed by policy support — PMAY subsidies, small loan sizes, strong repayment behaviour in target areas. If it's partly reclassification, the opportunity is real but smaller. Either way, lenders without distribution in smaller towns are watching this from the sidelines. Business correspondents, co-lending partnerships with HFCs, and agent networks are the access layer that makes this segment reachable.
